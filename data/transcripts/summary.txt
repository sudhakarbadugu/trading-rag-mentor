KEY IDEA 1 – CONTEXT IS EVERYTHING  
Why it matters: Every trade must be viewed inside the larger market‑trend, earnings‑growth, and sector‑dynamic picture. Without context a good chart pattern can be a trap.  
How to apply: Start each analysis with the “satellite view” (overall market trend), then zoom to “aerial view” (sector/industry), finally to “overhead view” (individual stock chart). Only move to the next level if the higher‑level context is favorable.
KEY IDEA 2 – CATEGORIZE COMPANIES BEFORE YOU BUY  
Why it matters: Different categories (market leader, top competitor, institutional favorite, turnaround, cyclicals, commodity) have different risk/reward profiles and require different entry criteria.  
How to apply: When a stock appears on your screen, first decide which bucket it belongs to. Use the bucket’s specific checklist (e.g., turnarounds need triple‑digit earnings growth; cyclicals need tighter risk limits).
KEY IDEA 3 – THE THREE CORE GOALS OF MINERVINI’S ENTRY PROCESS  
Why it matters: The whole system is built to (1) minimize risk, (2) capture outsized upside, (3) compound returns efficiently.  
How to apply:  
1. Set a stop‑loss that limits loss to a small % of your account (typically 1‑2%).  
2. Look for stocks that can move many times the risk amount (high “leverage” from price action, not from margin).  
3. Turn capital over quickly – don’t stay in cash longer than necessary, but also don’t be over‑exposed (Mark is ~50% invested on average).
KEY IDEA 4 – HARD FILTERS THAT NEVER BREAK  
Why it matters: The 200‑day moving average (MA) rule alone blocks >99% of losers. It is non‑negotiable.  
How to apply: Never buy a stock whose 200‑day MA is sloping down or whose price is below that MA. If you see a breakout but the 200‑day is down, walk away.
KEY IDEA 5 – RELATIVE STRENGTH (RS) AS A QUICK SCREEN  
Why it matters: 90% of historic winners have RS > 68 (IBD‑style). Low RS stocks rarely become big winners.  
How to apply: Use the built‑in RS indicator on Minervini Markets 360 (or IBD RS). Reject any candidate below 68.
KEY IDEA 6 – FUNDAMENTAL QUALIFIERS THAT ARE NOT NEGOTIABLE  
Why it matters: Strong earnings acceleration, small float, recent IPO, and a clear catalyst are the statistical hallmarks of winners.  
How to apply:  
If any of these is missing, lower the stock’s priority.
KEY IDEA 7 – STYLE DRIFT IS THE REAL KILLER  
Why it matters: Jumping from market leaders to cyclicals to “hot” strategies erodes skill development.  
How to apply: Commit to one style (e.g., market‑leader/turnaround) for at least 6‑12 months. Use a checklist to keep you from buying a “trend” that doesn’t fit your chosen category.
KEY IDEA 8 – MINIMAL USE OF MARGIN  
Why it matters: Mark’s 40‑year record shows massive returns with little or no margin. Leverage should come from high‑alpha stocks, not borrowed money.  
How to apply: Trade with cash or a small, judicious margin line only on intraday contests. For daily/weekly trading, keep margin at 0%.

SECTION 1: STUDENT NOTES (Clear, Simple, Actionable)
KEY IDEA 1 – VALUATION IS SECONDARY FOR HIGH‑GROWTH LEADERS  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Fast‑growing market leaders can trade at sky‑high P/E ratios that look “overvalued” but still have huge upside.  Trying to calculate a “fair” price often misleads you.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Focus on price action, growth momentum and chart criteria first.  Use valuation only as a sanity check after the trade is set up.
KEY IDEA 2 – THE CHEAP TRAP  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Buying a stock just because it is cheap (low P/E, low price) can lock you into a falling leader that keeps getting cheaper, pulling you deeper into loss.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Avoid stocks that are below their 200‑day moving average or in a downtrend, even if the P/E looks attractive.  Look for strong relative strength instead.
KEY IDEA 3 – P/E MOVES WITH EARNINGS, NOT PRICE  
WHY IT MATTERS:  When a stock rallies fast, earnings often grow faster, causing the P/E to fall even as the price rises.  The opposite happens on the downside.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Watch the earnings trend.  A falling P/E during a price rise usually signals accelerating earnings – a good sign.  A rising P/E during a price fall often signals earnings lagging price.
KEY IDEA 4 – FOUR STAGE CYCLE (WYCKOFF/STAN WEINSTEIN)  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Only one stage – Stage 2 (uptrend above the 200‑day) – produces the best compounding returns.  Stages 1, 3, 4 waste time or lose value.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Screen for stocks that are in Stage 2: price above 200‑day, 200‑day itself sloping up for at least 30 days, and relative strength >90 (ideally >95).  Stay out of Stage 1 (flat), Stage 3 (top/sideways), Stage 4 (downtrend).
KEY IDEA 5 – 50/80 RULE (Broken Leader Syndrome)  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Historically, about half of leading stocks will later drop 80 % and the other half will drop 50 % after a big rally.  Expect large pull‑backs; don’t assume a cheap dip is a buying opportunity.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Use tight stop‑losses and position sizing that can survive a 50 % move.  Be ready to exit if the stock falls below the 200‑day or shows a breakdown pattern.
KEY IDEA 6 – INSTITUTIONAL SUPPORT IS CRUCIAL  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Retail buying can only move a stock briefly.  Sustainable uptrends need large, persistent institutional buying.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Look for high volume spikes on up days, institutional block trades, and a clean, rising 200‑day.  Avoid stocks that rely on retail hype alone.
KEY IDEA 7 – RISK IS ALWAYS 100 % FROM ENTRY  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Even a stock that looks “cheap” can go to zero.  The only thing that protects you is proper risk management.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Never risk more than 1‑2 % of account equity on a single trade.  Set stop‑losses at a level that limits loss to that amount, regardless of how attractive the entry looks.
KEY IDEA 8 – BUY THE RIGHT PRICE, NOT THE LOWEST  
WHY IT MATTERS:  The optimal entry is just as the stock begins to move up, not the absolute low.  Buying at the low often means you’re already in a downtrend.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Identify the “base” formation (VCP, cup‑with‑handle, flat base) that is breaking out above the 200‑day.  Enter on the breakout, not after the stock has already fallen.
KEY IDEA 9 – REFERENCE POINTS & NORMAL VS ABNORMAL ACTION  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Knowing what normal price/volume behavior looks like lets you spot abnormal moves that signal danger.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Keep a checklist of normal patterns (steady uptrend, expanding volume on up days).  If you see a sudden gap down, extreme volume on a sell, or the 200‑day turning down, treat it as abnormal and reduce exposure.
KEY IDEA 10 – CONTINUOUS LEARNING & FRAMEWORK  
WHY IT MATTERS:  The market changes; a solid framework (stage analysis, moving‑average rule, RS filter) keeps you disciplined.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Follow the daily routine, review each trade against the framework, and adjust only when the framework tells you to.  Do not let emotions or “gut feelings” override the rules.

KEY IDEA 5 – THREE WAYS A COMPANY GROWS EARNINGS  
1) Sell more units  
2) Raise prices  
3) Cut costs (margin improvement) – limited in duration.  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Sustainable growth comes from #1 and #2 (revenue growth).  
HOW TO APPLY:  Prioritize stocks with strong revenue growth; treat cost cuts as a short‑term boost only.
KEY IDEA 6 – MATURATION CYCLE & STAGE CONCEPTS  
STAGE 2 – Early uptrend, forming a base (VCP‑type contraction).  
STAGE 3 – Mid‑trend, growth still strong but beginning to slow.  
STAGE 4 – Late trend, earnings decelerating, price often below 200‑day.  
WHY IT MATTERS:  The best entry is at the start of Stage 2; exit before Stage 4.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Identify the base, buy on the breakout, and set a stop just below the base.
KEY IDEA 7 – POST‑EARNINGS DRIFT & ESTIMATE REVISIONS  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Analysts revise estimates slowly; a strong earnings surprise can keep the stock rising for weeks.  
HOW TO APPLY:  After a big positive surprise, stay in the trade if price holds above the breakout level; watch for a reversal in volume.
KEY IDEA 8 – DIFFERENTIAL DISCLOSURE (PRICE VS. FUNDAMENTALS)  
WHY IT MATTERS:  A huge earnings beat followed by a massive sell‑off signals that institutions are dumping despite the numbers.  
HOW TO APPLY:  If price moves opposite the earnings story (big down‑volume on a beat), stay out.
KEY IDEA 9 – CODE MATRIX (ACCELERATION SCORE)  
CODE 33 = 3 quarters of EPS acceleration + 3 quarters of revenue acceleration + 3 quarters of margin expansion (ideal).  
CODE 32, 23, etc. = partial combos.  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Higher code → higher probability of a big move.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Rank your watchlist by code; prioritize CODE 33 and CODE 32 stocks.
KEY IDEA 10 – DISCIPLINE & BELIEF ARE THE REAL EDGE  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Everyone has information; only disciplined traders stick to the plan and compound returns.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Follow the daily checklist, respect stop‑losses, and keep a trade journal to reinforce the process.

KEY IDEA 1 – ONLY TRADE IN A LONG‑TERM UPTREND
Why it matters: If the market or the stock is not in a confirmed uptrend, all other fundamentals become irrelevant – you will likely lose money.
How to apply: Start every scan by checking the 200‑day moving average (or higher) on the index and on the individual stock. If the price is below that line, discard the ticker.
KEY IDEA 2 – USE A HIERARCHY OF QUALIFIERS
Why it matters: A step‑by‑step filter removes weak candidates and leaves only the strongest setups.
How to apply: Follow the three‑step order:
1. Long‑term trend (uptrend on the index and on the stock).
2. Current price action (higher lows, divergence from the market, new‑high dots).
3. Low‑risk entry (breakout of a base, pull‑back to a support zone, or a “follow‑through” day).
KEY IDEA 3 – NEW HIGHS ARE A POSITIVE SIGNAL, NOT A WARNING
Why it matters: A stock cannot make a large move without creating new all‑time highs. The money that fuels big moves lives on those highs.
How to apply: Look for black dots (all‑time highs) on the chart. When a stock is making a new high while the market is still low, it is a leader. Buy on the breakout or on a small pull‑back to the breakout level.
KEY IDEA 4 – FOCUS ON MARKET LEADERS DURING CORRECTIONS
Why it matters: 96 % of the biggest winners emerge from market corrections. Leaders diverge from the market, make higher lows, and eventually break out.
How to apply: Scan for stocks that:
– Are near or at 52‑week highs (within 5‑15 % of the high).
– Have a Relative Strength (RS) line that is flat or rising while the market is falling.
– Show higher lows while the index is making lower lows.
KEY IDEA 5 – PREDICTIVE, COINCIDENT, CONFIRMING SETUPS
Why it matters: Timing the entry depends on how early the leader appears.
How to apply:
– Predictive: Stock breaks out before the market bottom. Enter as soon as the base breakout is confirmed.
– Coincident: Stock breaks out around the same time the market makes a low. Use a “follow‑through” day as confirmation.
– Confirming: Stock breaks out after the market has already turned. Wait for a second day of higher volume to confirm.
KEY IDEA 6 – RELATIVE STRENGTH IS YOUR COMPASS
Why it matters: RS isolates the stock’s performance from the market. A rising RS while the market is down signals true strength.
How to apply: Plot the RS line (stock price divided by index price). Look for a line that is flat or trending up, especially when the index is trending down.
KEY IDEA 7 – POSITION SIZE & RISK CONTROL
Why it matters: Even the best setups can fail. Limiting loss protects capital and keeps you in the game.
How to apply:
– Max loss per trade ≤ 1 % of total account.
– Stop‑loss just below the recent swing low or below the base support.
– Reduce size on cyclical or less‑understood categories (biotech, turn‑arounds) until you have proven the pattern.
KEY IDEA 8 – DISCIPLINE OVER EMOTION
Why it matters: Undisciplined traders stay in losing positions, miss the next leader, and erode confidence.
How to apply: When a trade hits the stop, exit immediately. Do not “average down.” Keep a journal of every trade and review it daily.
KEY IDEA 9 – LEVERAGE COMES FROM BUYING ON NEW HIGHS
Why it matters: Buying a leader as it makes a new high gives you built‑in upside; the stock can move many multiples of the initial risk.
How to apply: After the breakout, let the trade run. Add to the position only if the stock makes a higher low and stays above the breakout level.
KEY IDEA 10 – CONTINUOUS LEARNING & REPEATS
Why it matters: The patterns repeat across decades (1990, 2000, 2008, 2020). Recognizing the repeat builds confidence.
How to apply: Study historic examples (Walmart 1982, Amazon 2001, Netflix 2008, NVIDIA 2020) and compare them to today’s charts. Keep a “pattern library” in your journal.

KEY IDEA 1 – PIVOT POINTS  
Why it matters: A pivot point is the price level where supply has been exhausted and demand begins to dominate. It often marks the low of a VCP base and gives the best entry with minimal risk.  
How to apply: After a stock has made a “turn” (break of a downtrend) watch the right‑hand side of the chart. When price contracts into a tight range and volume drops to a “vacuum,” draw a horizontal line at the low of that range – that is your pivot point.  
KEY IDEA 2 – THE TURN  
Why it matters: The turn signals the start of a new intermediate up‑trend. Buying before the turn is buying a down‑trend; buying after the turn aligns you with the new trend.  
How to apply: Confirm the turn by a clear break above the prior down‑trend line, followed by a pull‑back that respects the new higher low. Only then start looking for a pivot.  
KEY IDEA 3 – VCP (VOLATILITY CONTRACTION PATTERN)  
Why it matters: VCP shows that supply is being removed through repeated pull‑backs and tighter price action. The tighter the contraction, the stronger the eventual breakout.  
How to apply: Count the number of pull‑backs (minimum two). Each pull‑back should be tighter than the previous and occur on lower volume. When the right side is very tight (often 1‑2 days of low volume) you are ready to buy at the pivot.  
KEY IDEA 4 – CHEAT, LOW CHEAT, LOAD CHEAT  
Why it matters: These are “early” pivot points that form inside the base, lower than the traditional handle. Buying at a cheat gives a lower entry and higher upside potential.  
How to apply: Identify a small, tight low inside the base (often in the middle third). If volume is low and price is near the line of least resistance, consider a partial position (half or quarter). Add more if a higher pivot or handle forms.  
KEY IDEA 5 – SUPPLY & DEMAND REFERENCE LINES  
Why it matters: The “line of most resistance” (left‑side supply) and the “line of least resistance” (right‑side demand) frame the trade. The farther the supply is from the pivot, the lower the risk.  
How to apply: Draw a horizontal line at the pivot. Look left – how many days of supply (price above the line) exist? Fewer days = less risk.  
KEY IDEA 6 – VOLUME VACUUM & EXPANSION  
Why it matters: Low volume during contraction shows weak supply; high volume on breakout confirms strong demand.  
How to apply: Verify that the last 2‑3 days of contraction have the lowest volume of the entire base. Then watch for a volume surge on the breakout (or within a few days).  
KEY IDEA 7 – SYMMETRY & TIME COMPRESSION  
Why it matters: A symmetric base (balanced left and right sides) indicates the market had time to absorb supply. Time‑compressed bases (right side rushed) often fail.  
How to apply: Compare the width of the left side (distance from high to pivot) with the right side (distance from pivot to breakout). Aim for roughly equal or right side slightly longer.  
KEY IDEA 8 – POSITION SIZING & RISK  
Why it matters: Even a perfect setup can fail; limiting loss protects capital for the next trade.  
How to apply: Risk no more than 1‑2% of account per trade. Place stop just below the pivot (or below the low of the most recent pull‑back). Adjust position size accordingly.  
KEY IDEA 9 – DAILY DISCIPLINE  
Why it matters: Consistency beats occasional brilliance.  
How to apply: Follow a pre‑market screen, a set of entry rules, and a post‑trade journal every day.  
KEY IDEA 10 – MINDSET  
Why it matters: Trading is a marathon; volatility is the enemy, alpha is the goal.  
How to apply: Focus on clean, low‑risk entries that move up daily (zero volatility). Avoid chasing, avoid over‑trading, and treat each trade as a “train” you board just before it leaves the station.

1. **Chart Cues**  
   - Look for a tight, horizontal consolidation (base) on the daily chart.  
   - Verify that the base is also in an uptrend on the weekly chart (200‑day MA sloping up).  
   - Identify the pivot point: the highest price of the right‑hand side of the base.  
2. **Price / Volume Behavior**  
   - Price breaks the pivot by a few pennies; volume may be low (dry) at the breakout.  
   - VRR should not be dramatically low; a modest uptick after the breakout is ideal.  
   - After breakout, watch for TBA: a 1‑3 % pull‑back that is quickly “caught” by buying.  
3. **Buy Points**  
   - Immediate entry as price trades above the pivot (intraday).  
   - If the breakout is very close to an all‑time high, you may wait a few seconds for confirmation, then enter.  
   - Add on the next pull‑back to the breakout level (often 0.5‑1 % lower than the entry).  
4. **Confirmation Signals**  
   - Tightening of the right‑hand side (each contraction narrower than the last).  
   - Volume drying up on the right side of the base, then spiking on the breakout.  
   - No large “squat” or “shakeout” that closes in the lower half of the day’s range.  
5. **Danger Signs / Invalidations**

KEY IDEA 1 – YOU CREATE YOUR OWN ODDS  
Why it matters:  In the market you never control the odds; you control the odds you get on your capital. Betting a small amount into a large pot (high odds) lets you survive more mistakes.  
How to apply:  Aim for a risk‑to‑reward of at least 2:1 (preferably 3:1). Size each trade so that a loss is a small % of your account (e.g., 1‑2%). This gives you “big odds” on your money.

Why it matters:  Trading larger when you’re winning and smaller when you’re losing protects you from ruin and lets profits fund bigger bets.  
How to apply:  Start with a pilot trade (e.g., 5‑6% of portfolio). If it wins, use the profit to double the next position size. Continue scaling up while the streak stays positive; scale back or exit when a loss occurs.
KEY IDEA 3 – “EARN THE RIGHT TO TRADE BIGGER”  
Why it matters:  You only add capital after a smaller trade proves the strategy works. This prevents throwing large sums into unproven setups.  
How to apply:  Treat every winning trade as a “ticket” that lets you add a larger position on the next setup. Do not increase exposure unless you have open or closed profit to finance the added risk.
KEY IDEA 4 – RISK OF RUIN & STREAKS  
Why it matters:  Even a system with a positive expectancy can be wiped out if you stay flat‑sized during a losing streak.  
How to apply:  Use position‑size formulas (e.g., Kelly, fixed‑fraction) that shrink exposure after losses. Recognize that 20‑30 consecutive losses are possible; be prepared to cut back to the minimum size.
KEY IDEA 5 – STOP‑TO‑BREAKEVEN & PARTIAL EXIT (ADD‑AND‑REDUCE)  
Why it matters:  Moving a stop to breakeven or selling a portion of a winning position locks in profit while keeping upside.  
How to apply:  When a trade moves 2‑3× your stop distance, sell 50% (or enough to cover the stop) and move the stop on the remaining shares to breakeven. You can then add more shares if the trend continues, keeping overall risk unchanged.
KEY IDEA 6 – PROXIMITY RISK & STAGGERED STOPS  
Why it matters:  A stop placed too close to current price can be hit by normal volatility or a gap, causing unnecessary exits.  
How to apply:  If a stock is volatile, set a wider stop or use two staggered stops (e.g., 4% and 8%). The average of the two equals your target risk but gives the trade room to breathe.
KEY IDEA 7 – EARNINGS & IMPLIED VOLATILITY  
Why it matters:  Earnings can cause large moves; knowing the market’s implied volatility helps size a profit cushion.  
How to apply:  Calculate IV% = (Call Premium + Put Premium) / Stock Price × 100. Aim for a profit cushion of at least 2‑3× IV before the earnings announcement. If IV = 5%, keep at least 10‑15% unrealized profit or reduce position size.
KEY IDEA 8 – PRIORITY SHIFT AS TRADE PROGRESSES  
Why it matters:  Your focus changes from “protect downside” → “protect breakeven” → “protect profit”.  
How to apply:  1) Enter trade – primary goal = stop loss at calculated % (risk). 2) After price moves past stop distance, move stop to breakeven. 3) Once profit exceeds your average gain (≈9‑10% for Minervini), protect that level (e.g., lock in 9% by moving stop to that point).
KEY IDEA 9 – TURNOVER IS GOOD IF IT’S PROFIT‑DRIVEN  
Why it matters:  High turnover from cutting losers and taking profits is a sign of disciplined risk management, not “churning”.  
How to apply:  Track each trade’s R‑multiple. If you’re consistently exiting losers early and letting winners run, turnover will be high and equity curve will stay smooth.
KEY IDEA 10 – MINDSET: “BET ON THE PROCESS, NOT THE STOCK”  
Why it matters:  Emotions cause over‑trading, holding losers, or adding to losers.  
How to apply:  Follow the written rules every day. When a rule says “reduce exposure after a loss,” do it automatically. Treat each trade as a data point, not a personal win or loss.

Key Idea: Trade only stocks that are in a confirmed Stage‑2 uptrend and are forming a tight VCP base.  
Why it matters: Stage‑2 uptrends have already shown strength; a VCP base indicates a low‑risk, high‑reward entry.  
How to apply it: Scan for stocks with higher‑than‑market relative strength, then look for a base where price contracts and volume dries up near the prior high.
Key Idea: Enter on a pivot breakout that occurs close to the previous high of the base.  
Why it matters: The closer the breakout is to the prior high, the less “overhead” (price distance to cover) you have, improving risk‑reward.  
How to apply it: Measure the distance from the breakout point to the prior high; prefer breakouts within the top 20‑30% of that range.
Key Idea: Use precise measurement of pivots to decide if a move is “normal” or excessive.  
Why it matters: Over‑extended pivots increase risk; a measured, normal‑sized pullback keeps the trade safer.  
How to apply it: Compare the pivot’s length to the stock’s recent volatility range; if it exceeds the typical range, treat it as higher risk.
Key Idea: Discipline is the single most important factor; never abandon your stop‑loss or risk rule.  
Why it matters: Even a perfect setup can fail; disciplined loss cutting protects capital and keeps you in the game.  
How to apply it: Pre‑define your stop (e.g., 1‑2% below entry), place it immediately, and exit the moment it is hit—no excuses.
Key Idea: Relative Strength (RS) is a filter for market context.  
Why it matters: In a weak market, a stock that is still outperforming (RS > 100) may be a better candidate than a stock that looks good in isolation.  
How to apply it: Compare the stock’s price action to the S&P 500 or a sector index; look for a RS line that stays above the market line.
Key Idea: Position sizing should follow the “Optimal F” (Kelly) principle.  
Why it matters: Proper sizing maximizes growth while limiting drawdowns.  
How to apply it: Estimate your edge (win rate × average win ÷ average loss) and use a Kelly‑based calculator to determine the % of capital per trade (usually 1‑2% for most traders).
Key Idea: Institutional buying is best seen through large, sudden volume spikes, not delayed fund‑ownership data.  
Why it matters: Real‑time volume clues reveal when big money is entering, confirming the strength of a breakout.  
How to apply it: Look for 10‑20M+ share spikes on a breakout day; treat those as institutional footprints.
Key Idea: Inside day vs. outside day definitions.  
Why it matters: Outside days show expanding volatility (potential breakout), inside days show consolidation (potential VCP formation).  
How to apply it: Identify days where today’s high/low are outside (or inside) yesterday’s range; use outside days as early breakout signals, inside days as VCP confirmation.
Key Idea: Market context matters – don’t be dogmatic. If the overall market is down 30% and a stock is only down 20% with strong RS, it may still be a good play.  
Why it matters: Relative performance can outweigh absolute price levels.  
How to apply it: Adjust your “near‑high” rule when the market is weak; a pivot 30% below the prior high may be acceptable if the stock’s RS is high.
Key Idea: Continuous learning and open‑mind are essential; even after years of success, keep reviewing trades and updating knowledge.  
Why it matters: Markets evolve; staying adaptable prevents complacency.  
How to apply it: Keep a trade journal, review weekly, and allocate time for study (books, webinars, platform updates).
