SECTION 1: STUDENT NOTES (Clear, Simple, Actionable)
================================================================================

KEY IDEA 1 – CATEGORIES & CATALYSTS  
Why it matters: The biggest moves come from a handful of stock types.  
How to apply: Focus your screen on four groups – Market Leader, Top Competitor, Institutional Favorite, Turn‑around. Treat commodity‑cyclical plays as secondary.

KEY IDEA 2 – VALUATION IS SECONDARY TO GROWTH  
Why it matters: A high price is justified when earnings and sales are exploding.  
How to apply: When a stock shows double‑digit earnings growth and expanding margins, ignore traditional P/E limits. Verify the growth will last at least 1‑2 quarters.

KEY IDEA 3 – TREND STAGE  
Why it matters: Only Stage 2 up‑trends give the fastest capital appreciation. Stage 4 is a money‑loser; Stage 1/2 wastes time.  
How to apply: Use moving averages (50‑day, 200‑day) and price‑action to confirm you are in Stage 2 before entering.

KEY IDEA 4 – FUNDAMENTALS BREAKDOWN  
Why it matters: Earnings, sales, and margins are the three pillars of a growth stock.  
How to apply: Look for rising earnings, increasing top‑line sales, and improving (or at least stable) margins. If margins are falling, demand higher sales growth.

KEY IDEA 5 – VOLATILITY CONTRACTION PATTERN (VCP)  
Why it matters: Every Minervini trade is entered on a VCP; it keeps you out of “bucking broncos” (high‑volatility, high‑risk moves).  
How to apply: Scan for tightening price ranges, shrinking daily range, and a sharp drop in volume. The tighter the contraction, the better the entry.

KEY IDEA 6 – PRIMARY BASE (MAGNITUDE PLAY)  
Why it matters: The first buyable base after an IPO (or after a major correction) offers the biggest upside.  
How to apply: Identify the first VCP that forms after the IPO or after a deep correction. Verify low volume, tight price action, and a clear breakout point.

KEY IDEA 7 – ONE‑TWO PUNCH ENTRY  
Why it matters: The “one” is a precise, low‑risk entry; the “two” is the larger move that follows.  
How to apply: Enter at the exact point where volume contracts and price tightens (the “one”). Let the breakout (the “two”) carry the bulk of the profit.

KEY IDEA 8 – PIVOT POINTS & MULTIPLE BUY POINTS  
Why it matters: A pivot is the moment the stock changes direction; multiple entry levels let you add to winners.  
How to apply: After the initial VCP breakout, watch for pull‑backs to the breakout level or to the next minor support. Add only if the pattern remains tight and volume stays low.

KEY IDEA 9 – RISK MANAGEMENT (THE CORE OF DAY 4)  
Why it matters: Even the best setups can fail; protecting capital is the difference between a trader and a gambler.  
How to apply: Set stop‑loss just below the most recent supply (the low of the VCP). Limit risk to ≤1 % of account per trade. Never chase a broken pattern.

KEY IDEA 10 – MINDSET & DISCIPLINE  
Why it matters: Trading success is 80 % psychology.  
How to apply: Trust what you see on the chart, not what you think you know. Stay calm during pull‑backs, avoid “fear of missing out,” and keep a trade journal.

SECTION 4: SETUP IDENTIFICATION GUIDE (Based on Transcript)
================================================================================

CHART CUES
- Look for a sharp decline followed by a series of tighter price swings (VCP).  
- Volume should drop dramatically during the contraction; a “volume vacuum.”  
- The low of the VCP becomes the supply zone; breakout occurs above the prior high.

PRICE / VOLUME BEHAVIOR
- Price range narrows: each successive bar’s high‑low spread is smaller.  
- Daily volume falls to a fraction (often <30 % of prior average).  
- On breakout, volume spikes sharply, confirming demand.

BUY POINTS
- Enter at the exact point where the last contraction bar closes near its low and volume is at its minimum.  
- If the breakout candle closes above the prior high, place a stop just below the VCP low.

CONFIRMATION SIGNALS
- Weekly chart shows the same VCP shape (price tightness + low volume).  
- Relative strength: stock is above the 200‑day MA and outperforms the market during a correction.  
- Pivot point: a clear change from down‑trend to up‑trend on the same bar that starts the contraction.

DANGER SIGNS / INVALIDATIONS
- Right‑side compression: price is already crowded near the breakout level with visible supply.  
- Volume does not contract (remains high) – indicates lingering supply.  
- Breakout fails: price closes below the breakout high on the next bar.  
- Stock is in Stage 4 downtrend (below 50‑day MA, making lower lows).  
- Multiple large gaps down after the VCP – suggests fundamental weakness.

SECTION 6: EXAMPLES (If Applicable)
================================================================================

1. YAHOO (1997) – Primary base after a deep decline, VCP with tight price action, volume vacuum. Bought at breakout, 8,000 % gain in ~2 years. Demonstrates power of early primary‑base entry.

2. AMAZON – Early primary base not perfect; Mark admits he would wait for a tighter setup today. Still yielded 78,000 % over 18 years, showing that even imperfect VCPs can work if trend stays strong.

3. STARBUCKS – Primary base formed after IPO, tight VCP, low volume, breakout to 3,200 % over 11 years. Shows that IPO‑adjacent bases can be huge winners.

4. OLLIE’S – Classic VCP with multiple iterations, low volume, breakout, then a “one‑two punch” of adding on pull‑backs. Resulted in >100 % gain in a few months.

5. LINKEDIN – IPO, deep correction, tight VCP, institutional accumulation (12 M shares). Breakout after earnings; used as a “number‑one pick” example.

6. SHOPIFY – Recent IPO, deep correction, tight VCP on weekly chart, breakout after market low – illustrates that the same principles apply to modern tech stocks.

7. SHAKE SHACK – Not a high‑growth company but still produced a clean VCP after IPO, 86 % move in 30 days. Shows that even modest fundamentals can produce short‑term magnitude moves.
