SECTION 1: STUDENT NOTES (Clear, Simple, Actionable)
================================================================================

KEY IDEA: Alpha = excess return above a benchmark (e.g., S&P 500)  
WHY IT MATTERS: It tells you whether a trade is truly adding value, not just moving with the market.  
HOW TO APPLY: Look for stocks that are in a strong up‑trend, have low volatility (high alpha, low beta) and avoid those that swing wildly up and down.

KEY IDEA: Beta = volatility relative to the market  
WHY IT MATTERS: High beta stocks can give big moves but also big losses.  
HOW TO APPLY: Prefer low‑beta stocks for low‑risk trading; use beta as a filter when you need more stability.

KEY IDEA: Every trade MUST have a stop‑loss.  
WHY IT MATTERS: Stops protect you from the inevitable pull‑backs that will otherwise wipe out a position.  
HOW TO APPLY: Set a stop based on a technical level (e.g., below the pivot point) OR a fixed dollar/percentage you are willing to lose. Never trade without a stop – Mark has never done so in 40 years.

KEY IDEA: Volatility Contraction Pattern (VCP) / Base formation  
WHY IT MATTERS: A VCP shows supply being squeezed out and demand building – the classic “horse in the corral” analogy.  
HOW TO APPLY: Identify a series of lower highs and higher lows (contractions). Each contraction must be tighter than the previous, volume should fall, and the final contraction should be in single‑digit percent range.

KEY IDEA: Base Types – Cheat, Low Cheat, Handle (upper third)  
WHY IT MATTERS: The location of the price within the base tells you how much supply is left on the left side.  
HOW TO APPLY:  
- Cheat = middle third – moderate supply left.  
- Low Cheat = lower third – more supply, higher risk.  
- Handle = upper third – minimal supply, strongest setup.

KEY IDEA: Pivot Point (the “high” of the contraction)  
WHY IT MATTERS: The pivot is the price level you will buy near and the reference for drawing the “line of least resistance.”  
HOW TO APPLY: Use the highest price of the last contraction as the pivot. Draw a line back through the base to see where supply sits.

KEY IDEA: Inside Day & Squat (relative to VCP)  
WHY IT MATTERS: Inside days show tightening volatility; a squat shows a breakout that fails to hold above the midpoint of the range.  
HOW TO APPLY: Look for inside days on the right side of a VCP as a sign of tightening. Avoid buying a squat – wait for a clean breakout above the midpoint.

KEY IDEA: Fractals = patterns within patterns (small VCP inside a larger VCP)  
WHY IT MATTERS: Multiple fractal layers increase the probability of a strong move because the contraction process is repeating on several scales.  
HOW TO APPLY: Scan for a small VCP that sits inside a larger VCP; treat it as a higher‑confidence setup.

KEY IDEA: Market Sentiment & Corrections  
WHY IT MATTERS: A very bullish market with many stocks breaking new highs often precedes a correction.  
HOW TO APPLY: Monitor the percentage of stocks above their 50‑day and 200‑day MAs. If the market is high but many stocks are breaking down, prepare to short or tighten stops.

KEY IDEA: Leverage = proportional upside & downside  
WHY IT MATTERS: Using margin doubles both gains and losses; it does not improve risk‑reward.  
HOW TO APPLY: Stay cash‑based until you can consistently double your account without leverage. If you use leverage, keep position size tiny and stop‑loss tight.

KEY IDEA: Screening & Watchlist  
WHY IT MATTERS: You cannot examine every ticker; a screen isolates stocks that meet the Stage‑2 up‑trend, 50‑day > 200‑day, and VCP criteria.  
HOW TO APPLY: Run the screen daily, then manually review the right‑side contraction, supply levels, and fractal quality before adding to a watchlist.

KEY IDEA: Timing of Earnings & Gaps  
WHY IT MATTERS: Earnings create extra volatility; a small gap (≤1‑2% above pivot) can still be a good entry if the VCP is intact.  
HOW TO APPLY: If a stock gaps up modestly after earnings and still shows a tight VCP, you may enter; avoid large gaps that indicate over‑reaction.

KEY IDEA: “Never trade without a stop” & “Never assume a perfect setup means you should pause”  
WHY IT MATTERS: Over‑confidence leads to missed opportunities or large losses.  
HOW TO APPLY: Treat every setup with the same disciplined process; even a “perfect” chart gets a stop and position sizing.

SECTION 2: MINERVINI TRADING CONCEPTS EXTRACTED
================================================================================

- Alpha: Excess return above benchmark; target high‑alpha, low‑volatility stocks.  
- Beta: Relative volatility; low beta preferred for low‑risk trades.  
- VCP (Volatility Contraction Pattern): Series of tightening price ranges with falling volume; core entry signal.  
- Base Stages: Stage‑2 up‑trend is the only time to buy; Stage‑1,‑3,‑4 are not entry zones.  
- Base Types: Cheat (mid third), Low Cheat (lower third), Handle (upper third).  
- Pivot Point: Highest price of the last contraction; buy near it, draw line of least resistance back through the base.  
- Inside Day: Current day’s high/low inside previous day’s range; sign of tightening.  
- Squat: Breakout that fails to stay above the midpoint of the range; usually a false breakout.  
- Fractals: Smaller VCPs nested inside larger VCPs; increase confidence.  
- Supply/Demand Analysis: Left‑side supply (previous lows) must be cleared before a strong move.  
- Relative Strength (RS): Prefer RS > 90; below 70 is a violation.  
- 50‑day vs 200‑day MA: 50‑day must be above 200‑day for a Stage‑2 up‑trend.  
- Leverage: Increases both upside and downside; use sparingly.  
- Position Sizing & Risk‑to‑Reward: Aim for >2:1 reward; keep max loss per trade low (often <2%).  
- Market Sentiment Indicators: % of stocks above 50‑day/200‑day MAs, divergence between market index and individual stocks.  
- Earnings Gap Rules: Small gaps (≤1‑2% above pivot) are acceptable; large gaps need extra caution.  
- Time Compression: Contractions should become shorter and tighter; final contraction in single‑digit percent.  
- Power Plays: Very short bases (10‑12 days) – a special, aggressive VCP variant.  
- Screening Criteria (the “95% Club”): Focus on the four groups of high‑performing stocks identified in Minervini’s research.  
- Stop‑Loss Discipline: Never trade without a stop; stop can be technical or dollar‑based.  
- No “perfect” setup exemption: Even flawless charts get a stop and proper sizing.

SECTION 3: TRADING RULES & DO'S/DON'TS
================================================================================

DO:
- Always place a stop‑loss before entering a trade.
- Use the pivot point as your entry reference; aim to buy within 1‑2% of it.
- Trade only when the stock is in a confirmed Stage‑2 up‑trend (50‑day > 200‑day, RS > 90).
- Look for multiple fractal layers; the more the better.
- Keep leverage minimal; prefer cash trades until you have a proven edge.
- Measure each contraction; ensure each is tighter and lower‑volume than the previous.
- Draw the line of least resistance from the pivot back through the base to assess left‑side supply.
- Maintain a risk‑to‑reward of at least 2:1 on every trade.
- Review the market’s overall sentiment; be ready to tighten stops when sentiment turns bearish.
- Keep a disciplined watchlist: add only after the screen and visual confirmation.

DON'T:
- Trade without a stop – never, ever.
- Enter a stock that is more than 8% below its 50‑day MA (except in a low‑cheat, high‑risk scenario).
- Chase a gap that is more than 2% above the pivot; wait for a tighter pull‑back.
- Use price‑to‑sales or deep fundamental analysis as a primary filter; focus on price/volume patterns.
- Assume a “perfect” VCP means you can skip risk management.
- Over‑think the exact number of days a contraction should last; focus on symmetry and tightening.
- Rely on VWAP or other lagging indicators as a primary entry trigger.
- Trade based solely on ticker symbols in the chat; follow the scheduled live‑trading day for symbol work.
- Use margin to “double” your upside without reducing downside proportionally.
- Ignore supply on the left side of a base; a large supply area requires tighter stops or smaller positions.

SECTION 4: SETUP IDENTIFICATION GUIDE (Based on Transcript)
================================================================================

CHART CUES
- Stage‑2 up‑trend: 50‑day MA above 200‑day MA, price above both.
- VCP: 3+ contractions, each lower high and higher low, volume decreasing each time.
- Final contraction in single‑digit percent range (often <5%).
- Inside day(s) on the right side of the VCP indicate tightening.
- Fractals: Smaller VCP patterns inside the larger one.

PRICE / VOLUME BEHAVIOR
- Price pulls back to a tight range near the pivot point.
- Volume spikes on breakout, then drops sharply during contraction.
- Low volume on the pivot (the “high” of the last contraction) is a good sign.
- Avoid stocks where volume spikes on the left side but stays high on the right (indicates lingering supply).

BUY POINTS
- Enter within 1‑2% of the pivot high, preferably in the upper third (handle) or middle third (cheat) of the base.
- If the breakout is only 1‑2% above pivot after earnings, it can still be a valid entry.
- For power plays, entry can be as close as 10‑12 days after the first contraction if the pattern is tight.

CONFIRMATION SIGNALS
- Inside day on the day of breakout or the day before.
- No new lower lows after the breakout; price stays above the midpoint of the range.
- Supply line drawn from pivot back through base shows minimal supply above that line.
- Relative Strength > 90 (or at least >70) and staying above that level.

DANGER SIGNS / INVALIDATIONS
- Gap up >2% above pivot without a tight VCP – likely a false breakout.
- Contraction that widens (megaphone effect) rather than tightens.
- Stock trading >8% below its 50‑day MA (unless you are deliberately taking a low‑cheat, high‑risk play).
- High beta (>1.5) with erratic price swings.
- Presence of large supply zones on the left side (multiple low‑cheat levels).
- Failure to keep volume decreasing through each contraction.
- Breakout that immediately falls back below the midpoint (a squat).

SECTION 5: PRACTICAL DAILY ACTION PLAN FOR ME
================================================================================

PRE‑MARKET CHECKLIST
- Run the Minervini screen for Stage‑2 up‑trend stocks (50‑day > 200‑day, RS > 90, price > 50‑day).
- Scan for VCP candidates: at least 3 contractions, decreasing volume, final contraction <5%.
- Review any overnight news, earnings releases, and macro sentiment (e.g., % of S&P stocks above 50‑day MA).
- Update watchlist: add stocks that meet visual VCP criteria and have a clear pivot point.
- Set alerts for inside days and large volume spikes on the right side of each VCP.

MARKET HOURS DISCIPLINE
- At market open, verify that any pre‑market entries still respect the stop‑loss rule.
- For each trade, place stop immediately (technical level just below the pivot or a fixed %/dollar amount you can afford to lose).
- Keep position size such that a single loss does not exceed 1‑2% of total account equity.
- Monitor volume and price action; if volume rises unexpectedly on the left side, consider tightening stop or exiting.
- Avoid chasing gaps larger than 2% above the pivot; wait for a pull‑back into the cheat/handle zone.
- Record entry price, stop level, target (minimum 2:1 reward), and rationale in a trade journal.

POST‑MARKET REVIEW TASKS
- Review each trade: Was the stop hit? Did the VCP hold? Was the fractal pattern present?
- Update the journal with lessons learned (e.g., supply assessment, timing of earnings).
- Re‑run the screen to see if any missed opportunities would have qualified.
- Adjust watchlist: remove stocks that failed the VCP or showed widening volatility.
- Check overall portfolio risk: ensure total exposure stays within your risk tolerance.

RISK CONTROL REMINDERS
- Never trade without a stop.
- Max loss per trade ≤2% of account.
- Keep leverage at 0% (cash) until you have a proven 2:1+ reward system.
- If a stock falls >8% below its 50‑day MA, exit or reduce position.
- Use the “line of least resistance” to gauge left‑side supply; if supply is heavy, cut position size.

MINDSET REINFORCEMENT (Mark style)
- Treat each trade as a probability game; focus on edge, not on being right 100% of the time.
- Visualize the “horse in the corral” – stay on the horse until it reaches the finish line.
- Accept that perfect setups still require discipline; never let ego override rules.
- Review the “ticker‑sense” concept: listen to market flow, but rely on chart patterns, not gut feelings alone.
- Remember the compounding edge: turn over your edge quickly, but safely.

- Situation: Stock makes successive higher highs during contraction → indicates a “wedging up” rather than a true base.
- Interpretation: Not a valid VCP; treat as a continuation of the up‑trend, not a base. Wait for a proper contraction with higher lows.

- High Tight Flag (Power Play): Very short base (10‑12 days), sharp breakout, minimal left‑side supply.
- VCP: Longer base (4‑6 weeks), multiple contractions, fractal layers.
- Action: Use Power Play rules for very short‑term aggressive trades; otherwise follow standard VCP entry.

EXAMPLE 3 – Inside Day on Right Side of VCP
- Inside day appears after the third contraction, volume is low.
- Confirmation: Tightening is continuing; good entry opportunity within 1‑2% of pivot.

EXAMPLE 4 – Gap Up After Earnings
- Stock gaps up 1.5% above pivot after earnings, VCP still tight.
- Decision: Acceptable entry; set stop just below the pivot, target at least 2× risk.

EXAMPLE 5 – Supply Line Analysis
- Draw line from pivot back through base; see a small supply cluster at $16.50.
- Result: Minimal supply, high probability breakout; proceed with a standard position size.
