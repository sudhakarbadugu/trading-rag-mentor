SECTION 1: STUDENT NOTES (Clear, Simple, Actionable)
================================================================================

Key Idea: Trade only stocks that are in a confirmed Stage‑2 uptrend and are forming a tight VCP base.  
Why it matters: Stage‑2 uptrends have already shown strength; a VCP base indicates a low‑risk, high‑reward entry.  
How to apply it: Scan for stocks with higher‑than‑market relative strength, then look for a base where price contracts and volume dries up near the prior high.

Key Idea: Enter on a pivot breakout that occurs close to the previous high of the base.  
Why it matters: The closer the breakout is to the prior high, the less “overhead” (price distance to cover) you have, improving risk‑reward.  
How to apply it: Measure the distance from the breakout point to the prior high; prefer breakouts within the top 20‑30% of that range.

Key Idea: Use precise measurement of pivots to decide if a move is “normal” or excessive.  
Why it matters: Over‑extended pivots increase risk; a measured, normal‑sized pullback keeps the trade safer.  
How to apply it: Compare the pivot’s length to the stock’s recent volatility range; if it exceeds the typical range, treat it as higher risk.

Key Idea: Discipline is the single most important factor; never abandon your stop‑loss or risk rule.  
Why it matters: Even a perfect setup can fail; disciplined loss cutting protects capital and keeps you in the game.  
How to apply it: Pre‑define your stop (e.g., 1‑2% below entry), place it immediately, and exit the moment it is hit—no excuses.

Key Idea: Relative Strength (RS) is a filter for market context.  
Why it matters: In a weak market, a stock that is still outperforming (RS > 100) may be a better candidate than a stock that looks good in isolation.  
How to apply it: Compare the stock’s price action to the S&P 500 or a sector index; look for a RS line that stays above the market line.

Key Idea: Position sizing should follow the “Optimal F” (Kelly) principle.  
Why it matters: Proper sizing maximizes growth while limiting drawdowns.  
How to apply it: Estimate your edge (win rate × average win ÷ average loss) and use a Kelly‑based calculator to determine the % of capital per trade (usually 1‑2% for most traders).

Key Idea: Institutional buying is best seen through large, sudden volume spikes, not delayed fund‑ownership data.  
Why it matters: Real‑time volume clues reveal when big money is entering, confirming the strength of a breakout.  
How to apply it: Look for 10‑20M+ share spikes on a breakout day; treat those as institutional footprints.

Key Idea: Inside day vs. outside day definitions.  
Why it matters: Outside days show expanding volatility (potential breakout), inside days show consolidation (potential VCP formation).  
How to apply it: Identify days where today’s high/low are outside (or inside) yesterday’s range; use outside days as early breakout signals, inside days as VCP confirmation.

Key Idea: Market context matters – don’t be dogmatic. If the overall market is down 30% and a stock is only down 20% with strong RS, it may still be a good play.  
Why it matters: Relative performance can outweigh absolute price levels.  
How to apply it: Adjust your “near‑high” rule when the market is weak; a pivot 30% below the prior high may be acceptable if the stock’s RS is high.

Key Idea: Continuous learning and open‑mind are essential; even after years of success, keep reviewing trades and updating knowledge.  
Why it matters: Markets evolve; staying adaptable prevents complacency.  
How to apply it: Keep a trade journal, review weekly, and allocate time for study (books, webinars, platform updates).

SECTION 2: MINERVINI TRADING CONCEPTS EXTRACTED
================================================================================

- Stage‑2 Uptrend: The second leg of a multi‑stage trend; price is above the 200‑day moving average and making higher highs/lows.  
- VCP (Volatility Contraction Pattern): A base where price volatility contracts and volume dries up, usually in three “floors” (initial rise, pullback, second rise).  
- Pivot Breakout: The point where price breaks above a recent high after a contraction; entry is taken at the breakout.  
- “Near‑High” Entry Rule: Prefer breakouts that occur close to the previous high of the base to reduce overhead.  
- Relative Strength (RS): Comparison of a stock’s price performance to the market or sector; used to gauge market‑wide context.  
- Inside Day / Outside Day: Inside day = today’s range inside yesterday’s; outside day = today’s range expands beyond yesterday’s.  
- Institutional Footprint: Large volume spikes (10M+ shares) indicating big‑money participation.  
- Optimal F (Kelly Formula): A mathematical method to calculate the optimal fraction of capital to risk per trade based on edge and win/loss ratio.  
- Risk‑Reward (R) Ratio: Measuring profit potential versus risk; e.g., 5R means potential profit is five times the risk.  
- Position Sizing: Determining how many shares/contracts to trade based on Optimal F or a fixed % of equity (typically 1‑2%).  
- Discipline & Stop‑Loss: Pre‑set stop levels, never move them, and cut losses immediately.  
- Market Context Adjustment: Flexibility in applying rules based on overall market strength/weakness.  
- Earnings/Revenue Considerations: Avoid stocks with weak earnings outlook or insufficient fundamental catalysts.  
- “Power Play” vs. “Regular Play”: Stronger setups (large bases, high RS, clear VCP) qualify as power plays; weaker setups are regular.  
- Psychological Resilience: Ability to stay disciplined after a series of losses or after a winning trade turns against you.  
- Platform Tools (Fab Five indicators, Minervini Markets 360): Technical screens that flag Stage‑2, VCP, RS, etc.

SECTION 3: TRADING RULES & DO'S/DON'TS
================================================================================

DO:
- Trade only stocks that are in a confirmed Stage‑2 uptrend.
- Look for VCP bases with three contraction “floors” and drying volume.
- Enter on a breakout that occurs close to the prior high of the base.
- Measure each pivot; accept only “normal” sized pullbacks.
- Use relative strength to filter stocks; prefer RS > market.
- Place a stop‑loss immediately (typically 1‑2% below entry) and honor it.
- Size each trade using Optimal F or no more than 1‑2% of capital.
- Track large volume spikes as evidence of institutional buying.
- Review trades daily; keep a detailed journal.
- Stay disciplined regardless of market conditions or recent trade outcomes.

DON’T:
- Enter on pivots that are far below the prior high without strong RS justification.
- Move your stop after the trade is live; never “average down” a losing position.
- Rely on delayed fund‑ownership data for entry decisions.
- Trade purely on a “feel” without measuring the setup.
- Ignore market context; don’t force a trade when the overall market is weak.
- Over‑trade small‑cap stocks with thin liquidity (e.g., $9‑price stocks) unless you have a clear edge.
- Let emotions dictate position size; never exceed your pre‑defined risk.
- Assume a single successful trade proves the system; keep testing every trade.
- Skip the pre‑market scan for Stage‑2/VCP candidates.
- Forget to update your knowledge; treat the methodology as static.

SECTION 4: SETUP IDENTIFICATION GUIDE (Based on Transcript)
================================================================================

Chart Cues:
- Stock is above its 200‑day moving average and making higher highs/lows → Stage‑2.
- Price forms a base with three distinct “floors” where each subsequent floor is higher than the previous low.
- Volume contracts on each floor and dries up on the final floor.
- The most recent high of the base is clearly visible; breakout should occur near this level.

Price/Volume Behavior:
- Dry‑up volume on the final contraction (e.g., volume < 50% of average daily volume).
- Large volume spike (10M+ shares) on breakout day → institutional participation.
- Inside days during the contraction phase; outside day on breakout.

Buy Points:
- Place entry order a few ticks above the breakout high (or at the high if you use a stop‑order).
- Confirm that the breakout is above the prior high by no more than 10‑20% of the base’s height (i.e., “near‑high”).

Confirmation Signals:
- Price closes above the breakout level on the same day or next day.
- Relative Strength line stays above the market line.
- No immediate reversal candle (e.g., no long lower shadow) after breakout.

Danger Signs / Invalidations:
- Pivot occurs more than 30‑40% below the prior high without strong RS.
- Volume on breakout is weak or declining.
- Inside day appears after breakout (sign of lack of follow‑through).
- Stop‑loss hit on the first day (means entry was too aggressive or setup was weak).
- Earnings or fundamental catalyst is negative or absent.

SECTION 5: PRACTICAL DAILY ACTION PLAN FOR ME
================================================================================

PRE‑MARKET CHECKLIST:
- Run the Fab Five / Minervini Markets 360 screen for Stage‑2 VCP candidates.
- Verify each candidate’s RS line is above the market index.
- Check upcoming earnings calendar; filter out stocks with negative earnings outlook.
- Note any large institutional volume spikes from pre‑market data.
- Set up watchlist with entry levels (breakout high + 1‑2 ticks) and stop‑loss levels (1‑2% below entry).

MARKET HOURS DISCIPLINE:
- At market open, scan for any overnight breakout on the watchlist; if none, wait for intraday VCP formation.
- When a breakout occurs, place entry order at the defined level; do not chase price.
- Immediately place stop‑loss as per pre‑defined rule.
- Monitor volume; if breakout volume is weak, consider sitting out.
- Keep a maximum of 3‑5 open positions at any time to avoid over‑exposure.
- Record entry price, stop, target (e.g., 5R), and rationale in trade journal.

POST‑MARKET REVIEW TASKS:
- Review each trade: entry, stop hit or target hit, and reason for outcome.
- Update journal with lessons learned; note any rule breaches.
- Re‑run the screen for the next day’s watchlist.
- Analyze any missed setups (e.g., VCP that didn’t break out) for future reference.
- Spend 30 minutes reading a Minervini chapter or a market‑analysis article.

RISK CONTROL REMINDERS:
- Never risk more than 1‑2% of total equity on a single trade.
- Keep stop‑loss distance consistent (percentage or ATR‑based).
- If you have 3 consecutive losing trades, pause and review discipline.
- Adjust position size if account equity changes (re‑calculate Optimal F weekly).

MINDSET REINFORCEMENT (Mark style):
- Remind yourself: “Discipline is the hallmark of greatness.”
- Visualize the worst‑case loss (stop hit) and accept it before entering.
- Treat each trade as a probability experiment, not a certainty.
- Celebrate small wins (e.g., hitting a 2R) but stay humble.
- Keep an “open mind” – be ready to adapt if market conditions shift.
