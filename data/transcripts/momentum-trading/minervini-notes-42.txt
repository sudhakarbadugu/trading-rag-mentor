SECTION 1: STUDENT NOTES (Clear, Simple, Actionable)
================================================================================

KEY IDEA 1 – YOU CREATE YOUR OWN ODDS  
Why it matters:  In the market you never control the odds; you control the odds you get on your capital. Betting a small amount into a large pot (high odds) lets you survive more mistakes.  
How to apply:  Aim for a risk‑to‑reward of at least 2:1 (preferably 3:1). Size each trade so that a loss is a small % of your account (e.g., 1‑2%). This gives you “big odds” on your money.

KEY IDEA 2 – PROGRESSIVE EXPOSURE (SCALING)  
Why it matters:  Trading larger when you’re winning and smaller when you’re losing protects you from ruin and lets profits fund bigger bets.  
How to apply:  Start with a pilot trade (e.g., 5‑6% of portfolio). If it wins, use the profit to double the next position size. Continue scaling up while the streak stays positive; scale back or exit when a loss occurs.

KEY IDEA 3 – “EARN THE RIGHT TO TRADE BIGGER”  
Why it matters:  You only add capital after a smaller trade proves the strategy works. This prevents throwing large sums into unproven setups.  
How to apply:  Treat every winning trade as a “ticket” that lets you add a larger position on the next setup. Do not increase exposure unless you have open or closed profit to finance the added risk.

KEY IDEA 4 – RISK OF RUIN & STREAKS  
Why it matters:  Even a system with a positive expectancy can be wiped out if you stay flat‑sized during a losing streak.  
How to apply:  Use position‑size formulas (e.g., Kelly, fixed‑fraction) that shrink exposure after losses. Recognize that 20‑30 consecutive losses are possible; be prepared to cut back to the minimum size.

KEY IDEA 5 – STOP‑TO‑BREAKEVEN & PARTIAL EXIT (ADD‑AND‑REDUCE)  
Why it matters:  Moving a stop to breakeven or selling a portion of a winning position locks in profit while keeping upside.  
How to apply:  When a trade moves 2‑3× your stop distance, sell 50% (or enough to cover the stop) and move the stop on the remaining shares to breakeven. You can then add more shares if the trend continues, keeping overall risk unchanged.

KEY IDEA 6 – PROXIMITY RISK & STAGGERED STOPS  
Why it matters:  A stop placed too close to current price can be hit by normal volatility or a gap, causing unnecessary exits.  
How to apply:  If a stock is volatile, set a wider stop or use two staggered stops (e.g., 4% and 8%). The average of the two equals your target risk but gives the trade room to breathe.

KEY IDEA 7 – EARNINGS & IMPLIED VOLATILITY  
Why it matters:  Earnings can cause large moves; knowing the market’s implied volatility helps size a profit cushion.  
How to apply:  Calculate IV% = (Call Premium + Put Premium) / Stock Price × 100. Aim for a profit cushion of at least 2‑3× IV before the earnings announcement. If IV = 5%, keep at least 10‑15% unrealized profit or reduce position size.

KEY IDEA 8 – PRIORITY SHIFT AS TRADE PROGRESSES  
Why it matters:  Your focus changes from “protect downside” → “protect breakeven” → “protect profit”.  
How to apply:  1) Enter trade – primary goal = stop loss at calculated % (risk). 2) After price moves past stop distance, move stop to breakeven. 3) Once profit exceeds your average gain (≈9‑10% for Minervini), protect that level (e.g., lock in 9% by moving stop to that point).

KEY IDEA 9 – TURNOVER IS GOOD IF IT’S PROFIT‑DRIVEN  
Why it matters:  High turnover from cutting losers and taking profits is a sign of disciplined risk management, not “churning”.  
How to apply:  Track each trade’s R‑multiple. If you’re consistently exiting losers early and letting winners run, turnover will be high and equity curve will stay smooth.

KEY IDEA 10 – MINDSET: “BET ON THE PROCESS, NOT THE STOCK”  
Why it matters:  Emotions cause over‑trading, holding losers, or adding to losers.  
How to apply:  Follow the written rules every day. When a rule says “reduce exposure after a loss,” do it automatically. Treat each trade as a data point, not a personal win or loss.

SECTION 2: MINERVINI TRADING CONCEPTS EXTRACTED
================================================================================

- ODDS ON YOUR MONEY: Seek favorable risk‑to‑reward; never bet a small amount into a large pot.  
- PROGRESSIVE EXPOSURE: Scale position size up with winners, down with losers.  
- EARN THE RIGHT TO TRADE LARGER: Use profits (open or closed) to finance bigger bets.  
- RISK OF RUIN: Understand that long loss streaks can destroy a flat‑sized account; adjust size accordingly.  
- VCP (VOLATILITY CONTRACTION PATTERN): Mentioned as a setup that can proliferate, prompting larger exposure.  
- ADD‑AND‑REDUCE (or “add and reduce”): Add shares while moving stop up so total risk stays the same.  
- REDUCE‑AND‑FREE‑ROLL: Sell part of a winning position to fund the stop, making the trade essentially risk‑free.  
- HALF‑SELL (50% exit): Provides a psychological win‑win and keeps risk‑reward intact.  
- PROXIMITY RISK: Risk of being stopped out by a tiny price move or gap when stop is too close.  
- STAGGERED STOPS: Two stops (tight and wide) whose average equals desired risk, giving the trade breathing room.  
- IMPLIED VOLATILITY (IV) FOR EARNINGS: Use option premiums to estimate expected move; set profit cushion accordingly.  
- POSITION SIZING RULES: Full position capped at ~25% of equity; pilot trades ~5‑6%; scale in steps (6.25%, 12.5%, 25%).  
- RISK‑REWARD PRIORITY SHIFT: Protect downside → protect breakeven → protect profit (average gain).  
- TURNOVER AS A POSITIVE SIGNAL: High turnover from disciplined exits is desirable.  
- PSYCHOLOGICAL DISCIPLINE: “Bet on the process, not the stock”; avoid ego‑driven size increases.

SECTION 3: TRADING RULES & DO'S/DON'TS
================================================================================

DO:
- Set a minimum risk‑to‑reward of 2:1 on every trade.
- Start each new market entry with a pilot size (≈5‑6% of equity).
- Use profits to finance larger subsequent positions.
- Move stops to breakeven once the trade is 2‑3× the stop distance.
- Sell half (or enough to cover the stop) when a trade is comfortably in profit.
- Adjust position size down after any loss; never increase size on a losing streak.
- Calculate implied volatility before earnings and keep a profit cushion of 2‑3× IV.
- Use staggered stops when the stock is volatile.
- Keep the full‑position cap at ~25% of total equity.
- Review your equity curve daily; aim for a smooth, upward trend.

DON’T:
- Lay odds (bet a small amount into a large pot) – always get odds on your money.
- Add to a losing position without first having open or closed profit.
- Keep the same flat size through a long losing streak.
- Choke a winning trade by moving the stop too close too early.
- Let proximity risk force a stop on a normal pullback; consider widening or staggered stops.
- Trade on intuition alone; follow the written process.
- Hold a full position into earnings without a profit cushion.
- Ignore the need to reduce exposure when market sentiment turns bearish.
- Let emotions dictate position size; always follow the scaling rules.

SECTION 4: SETUP IDENTIFICATION GUIDE (Based on Transcript)
================================================================================

CHART CUES
- VCP or “cheat area” (tight consolidation in the lower third of the price range) followed by a breakout.
- Formation of a “handle” after the breakout – indicates continuation potential.
- Low‑volume pullbacks that respect the 20‑day and 50‑day moving averages.
- Volume spikes on breakout; low volume on normal pullbacks.

PRICE / VOLUME BEHAVIOR
- Initial breakout: price moves ≥2× the stop distance with rising volume.
- After breakout, price may retrace 10‑20% (normal pullback) before resuming higher.
- Look for multiple lower lows that stay above key moving averages – sign of strength.

BUY POINTS
- Enter at the top of the cheat area or just above the breakout point.
- Use a stop 4‑6% below entry (or based on technical support) as the pilot trade.
- If the trade hits the target (2‑3× stop), consider adding more shares (add‑and‑reduce).

CONFIRMATION SIGNALS
- Price holds above the breakout level for at least one full day.
- Volume on the second day remains above the average of the last 20 days.
- The trade moves to a new 20‑day high without violating the stop.

DANGER SIGNS / INVALIDATIONS
- Immediate reversal that hits the stop within the first 30‑60 minutes.
- Large gap down that bypasses the stop (proximity risk) – exit or reduce exposure.
- Failure to respect the 20‑day/50‑day support after a pullback.
- Earnings announcement approaching without a sufficient profit cushion (IV‑based).

SECTION 5: PRACTICAL DAILY ACTION PLAN FOR ME
================================================================================

PRE‑MARKET CHECKLIST
- Review overnight news, earnings calendar, and implied volatility for any holdings.
- Identify 3‑5 VCP or cheat‑area setups on the watchlist; note entry, stop, and target.
- Calculate pilot position size (≈5‑6% of equity) for each candidate.
- Verify that any planned trade has a minimum 2:1 risk‑reward.
- Check account equity; ensure full‑position cap (≤25%) is not exceeded.

MARKET HOURS DISCIPLINE
- 8:30‑9:30 am: Execute only pilot trades that meet all criteria.
- After each pilot win: Immediately log profit, calculate new position size, and plan the next add‑on.
- If a trade hits stop: Reduce exposure to the minimum pilot size before the next entry.
- Throughout the day: Monitor stop levels; adjust to breakeven once price ≥2× stop distance.
- Use staggered stops for volatile stocks; set primary stop tighter, secondary wider.

POST‑MARKET REVIEW TASKS
- Record R‑multiple of every trade (win or loss).
- Update the “exposure ledger” showing how profits funded larger positions.
- Analyze any proximity‑risk events; decide if stop width needs adjustment.
- Review equity curve for smoothness; note any drawdown spikes and why they occurred.
- Plan the next day’s pilot trades based on today’s outcomes.

RISK CONTROL REMINDERS
- Never exceed 25% total exposure across all open positions.
- Keep each individual trade risk ≤2% of total equity (adjust if equity changes).
- After any loss, reset next trade to pilot size regardless of confidence.
- Use profit‑cushion (≥2× IV) before holding through earnings.
- If a stop is within 0.5% of current price and volatility is high, widen or stagger the stop.

MINDSET REINFORCEMENT (Mark style)
- “Bet on the process, not the stock.” Recite before each trade.
- Visualize the trade as a data point; avoid personal attachment.
- Remind yourself: “I earn the right to trade bigger only after I’ve proven the smaller trade.”
- Celebrate each successful scaling step; treat a loss as a data point, not a failure.
- Review the “odds” you have on your money each morning; if odds are low, stay out.

SECTION 6: EXAMPLES (If Applicable)
================================================================================

EXAMPLE 1 – ADD‑AND‑REDUCE
- Buy 1,000 shares @ $16.50, stop $1 below entry (risk $1,000).  
- Stock moves to $18.50 (≈2× stop). Add 1,000 more shares at $18.00, move stop on entire 2,000‑share position to $17.00.  
- Total risk remains $1,000, upside doubled.

EXAMPLE 2 – HALF‑SELL FOR WIN‑WIN
- Position up 30% with 2,000 shares.  
- Sell 1,000 shares (50%) to lock in profit and finance the stop on the remaining 1,000.  
- If price continues up, you still have exposure; if it reverses, you keep the profit already taken.

EXAMPLE 3 – IMPLIED VOLATILITY CALCULATION
- Stock $162, nearest call $160 ask = $3.50, nearest put $160 ask = $2.75.  
- IV% = (3.50 + 2.75) / 162 × 100 ≈ 3.86%.  
- Set profit cushion ≥ 2 × 3.86% ≈ 8% before earnings.

EXAMPLE 4 – STAGGERED STOP
- Desired risk = 6%.  
- Set primary stop at 4% (tight) and secondary stop at 8% (wide).  
- Average = (4% + 8%) / 2 = 6% risk, giving the trade extra breathing room.
