SECTION 1: STUDENT NOTES (Clear, Simple, Actionable)
================================================================================

KEY IDEA 1 – PIVOT POINTS  
Why it matters: A pivot is the exact price where a breakout becomes “buyable.” If the stock fails to stay above the pivot it is a false breakout.  
How to apply: Wait for the price to cross the pivot and close above it (or hit the pivot on a pull‑back). Only enter at the pivot or a few cents below; do not chase a stock that has already spiked 10% and fallen back.

KEY IDEA 2 – VOLATILITY CONTRACTION PATTERN (VCP)  
Why it matters: VCP shows a tightening price range and decreasing volume, indicating supply is being exhausted. This is the “low‑volatility” entry zone.  
How to apply: Look for a series of tightening pull‑backs (usually 3‑5). The final pull‑back should be the tightest and occur near the right side of the base. Enter at the breakout from that tight area.

KEY IDEA 3 – CHEATS, LOW CHEATS, HANDLES, POWER PLAYS  
Why it matters: Different stages of a base have different risk/reward profiles. Low cheats are early, high‑risk; cheats and handles are more reliable; power plays are rare but can be huge.  
How to apply:  
- Low cheat: buy if the setup meets all VCP criteria even without a handle.  
- Cheat: buy after the low cheat if the price contracts further.  
- Handle: buy after the cheat when a clear handle forms and the breakout clears all supply.  
- Power play: a very short, tight base (often <2 weeks) that breaks out with strong volume.

KEY IDEA 4 – SCREENING TIME MANAGEMENT  
Why it matters: Efficient screening lets you focus on high‑probability setups without wasting hours.  
How to apply:  
- Weekends: 2‑3 hours of heavy screening (full scan, flag potential bases).  
- Weekdays: 30 minutes to 1 hour pre‑market to review flagged names, news, earnings.  
- Keep a “watch‑list” (buyable now) and a “buy‑list” (needs confirmation).

KEY IDEA 5 – RISK MANAGEMENT & STOP‑LOSS PLACEMENT  
Why it matters: Protecting capital is more important than catching every big winner.  
How to apply:  
- Place a hard stop as soon as you buy, typically 4‑6 % below the entry (or just below the pivot low).  
- Move stop to break‑even once the trade is 5‑6 % in profit.  
- Use staggered stops only in highly volatile markets to keep half the position alive.  
- Do NOT use trailing stops that trail too tightly; they get “whipped” in whipsaw markets.

KEY IDEA 6 – PROGRESSIVE EXPOSURE (ADDING POSITION SIZE)  
Why it matters: When a trade works, you should increase exposure quickly; when it fails, cut back fast.  
How to apply:  
- Start with a quarter position.  
- If the trade moves +5‑6 % and other setups are also working, add another quarter (or a half).  
- If the trade stalls or market pressure rises, stop adding and consider reducing.  
- Base the decision on “traction” – how many setups are working, how the market feels, and your current portfolio heat.

KEY IDEA 7 – OPEN vs CLOSED PROFITS & “LEANING ON PROFIT”  
Why it matters: Open profit can be used as “capital” to fund new trades without adding fresh cash. Closed profit can be used to increase overall exposure.  
How to apply:  
- When a trade is +10 % and you have a 2 % average gain target, you may sell half, lock in profit, and use that cash to fund a new breakout.  
- Never let an open profit turn into a loss by holding a losing position too long; protect the gain with a stop.

KEY IDEA 8 – MARKET TREND IDENTIFICATION  
Why it matters: The overall market must be in an up‑trend for SEPA to work.  
How to apply:  
- 50‑day MA above 200‑day MA, and 200‑day MA trending up.  
- S&P 500 pull‑backs limited to 2‑5 % (shallow).  
- If the market is choppy or the VIX is high, reduce exposure and focus on tighter VCPs.

KEY IDEA 9 – FUNDAMENTALS ARE SECONDARY  
Why it matters: The chart is the final arbiter. Good earnings, sales, or news do not override a broken VCP.  
How to apply: Check earnings and news for context, but if the price action fails the technical criteria, stay out.

KEY IDEA 10 – PSYCHOLOGY & DISCIPLINE  
Why it matters: The edge comes from consistent rule‑following, not from “getting lucky.”  
How to apply:  
- Treat every trade as a probability game (like poker).  
- Accept that you will miss some leaders and catch some losers.  
- Stick to the plan; do not change it mid‑trade because of “market pressure.”  
- Review trades daily, keep a loss‑limit (1‑2 % of equity per trade), and never risk more than your total risk budget.

SECTION 2: MINERVINI TRADING CONCEPTS EXTRACTED
================================================================================

- VCP (Volatility Contraction Pattern): tightening price action with decreasing volume, usually 3‑5 pull‑backs, ending in a tight breakout.  
- Pivot Point: the exact price level where supply has been cleared; entry should be at or just below this level.  
- Low Cheat / Cheat / Handle: three progressive stages of a base; each has its own buy point.  
- Power Play: very short, tight base (often <2 weeks) that breaks out sharply; rare but high‑reward.  
- Trend Template: 50‑day above 200‑day, 200‑day sloping upward, shallow market pull‑backs.  
- Relative Strength (RS): look for RS > 90 (or 95) to confirm a leader; lower RS may still be acceptable if other criteria are strong.  
- Earnings & Sales Requirements: earnings acceleration and strong sales are supportive but never override a broken VCP.  
- Position Sizing Rules: start with ¼ of intended max position; add in quarters as the trade proves itself.  
- Risk Management: hard stop placed 4‑6 % below entry (or just below pivot low); stop moved to break‑even after 5‑6 % gain.  
- Staggered Stops: split position into two halves with separate stops to survive whipsaws.  
- Open vs Closed Profit: open profit can be “leaned on” for new trades; closed profit funds overall exposure.  
- Trailing Stops: rarely used; only on parabolic moves, otherwise mental stops.  
- Psychology: treat trading like poker – focus on odds, not on individual outcomes.  
- Screening Process: weekend heavy screen (2‑3 h), weekday quick scan (30‑60 min).  
- Market Volatility Assessment: use VIX for overall market, but primary gauge is how many stocks are forming tight VCPs.  
- Ants (12 of 15 days up): early‑stage strength indicator; may signal a good entry or a warning if the move is already extended.  
- “Lean on Traction”: use the number of winning setups and portfolio heat to decide when to increase exposure.  
- “Backstop” (50‑day rule): sell or move stop if price closes below 50‑day for three consecutive weeks (or strong volume down).  
- “Power Factor”: compounding effect of large gains; larger gains require less additional return to improve overall equity.

SECTION 3: TRADING RULES & DO'S/DON'TS
================================================================================

DO:
- Wait for the price to cross the pivot point before buying.
- Place a hard stop as soon as you enter, typically 4‑6 % below entry or just below the pivot low.
- Move stop to break‑even once the trade is 5‑6 % in profit.
- Use staggered stops in high‑volatility environments.
- Add to a winning trade only after it has moved 5‑6 % in your favor.
- Keep total risk per trade ≤ 2 % of total equity.
- Review the watch‑list daily and remove stocks that fail the VCP criteria.
- Use the 50‑day above 200‑day trend as a market‑wide filter.
- Treat each trade as a probability game; accept losses as part of the process.
- Keep a “sold‑list” of stocks you exited to watch for re‑entries.

DON'T:
- Chase a stock that has already spiked 10 % and fallen back; the breakout is no longer clean.
- Use trailing stops that trail too tightly; they will be hit in normal whipsaws.
- Add position size in a market that is choppy or when you feel “pressure” – let the rules dictate exposure.
- Rely on the VIX or any single indicator to decide on a trade; focus on the chart.
- Base decisions on who is buying (e.g., “who is buying this gap?”) – the chart tells you the answer.
- Ignore the 50‑day rule; do not hold a stock that closes below the 50‑day for three weeks without a strong reason.
- Over‑rely on fundamentals; if the price action fails, stay out.
- Change your trade plan mid‑trade because of news or market sentiment unless the stop is hit.
- Trade without a pre‑defined stop; never “hope” a loss will reverse.

SECTION 4: SETUP IDENTIFICATION GUIDE (Based on Transcript)
================================================================================

CHART CUES
- Look for a base (VCP) with 3‑5 tightening pull‑backs.
- The final pull‑back should be the tightest and sit on the right side of the base.
- Volume should decline on each pull‑back and then surge on the breakout.
- Pivot point appears as a clear low‑price level where the breakout clears all prior supply.

PRICE / VOLUME BEHAVIOR
- Tight price range (e.g., 0.5‑1 % swing) over several weeks.
- Decreasing daily volume on each pull‑back.
- Breakout volume at least 2‑3× the average of the last 20 days.
- After breakout, price should stay above the pivot for at least one day (or close above on the same day).

BUY POINTS
- Enter at the exact pivot price or a few cents below it.
- For a low cheat, buy as soon as the price rebounds to the cheat level and shows tightening.
- For a handle, wait for the handle to form and break above the handle high.

CONFIRMATION SIGNALS
- Close above the pivot on the breakout day.
- No immediate reversal (price stays above pivot for at least one additional bar).
- Relative Strength > 90 (optional but helpful).
- No large sell‑off volume on the breakout day.

DANGER SIGNS / INVALIDATIONS
- Price spikes > 10 % then falls back below the pivot on the same day.
- Volume spikes on the pull‑back (indicates new supply).
- Breakout fails to close above the pivot (close below or at the pivot).
- Three consecutive lower lows or weekly close below the 50‑day MA.
- Market VIX spikes and many stocks are failing VCPs (high overall volatility).

SECTION 5: PRACTICAL DAILY ACTION PLAN FOR ME
================================================================================

PRE‑MARKET CHECKLIST
- Run the weekend heavy screen; flag any new VCPs, cheats, handles, power plays.
- Update the “watch‑list” with symbols that have cleared the pivot overnight.
- Review earnings calendar for the day; note any stocks with earnings within 24 h.
- Check macro news (interest rates, major geopolitical events) for market‑wide risk.
- Set alerts for pivot points and stop‑loss levels on all watch‑list stocks.
- Confirm the market trend: 50‑day > 200‑day and 200‑day sloping up.

MARKET HOURS DISCIPLINE
- Monitor price action on the watch‑list; only enter when price hits the pivot.
- Place hard stop immediately after entry; log the stop level in your trade journal.
- Move stop to break‑even once the trade is +5 % to +6 % profit.
- If a trade moves +10 %–+15 %, consider adding a quarter position (progressive exposure).
- Keep a running total of portfolio risk; stop adding new positions if total risk > 10 % of equity.
- Use staggered stops only if the market shows high intraday volatility (wide ATR).

POST‑MARKET REVIEW TASKS
- Close out any positions that hit their stop or reached your profit target.
- Record each trade: entry, exit, stop level, reason for exit, profit/loss.
- Update the “sold‑list” with symbols you exited; watch them for re‑entry signals.
- Review the market trend: did the 50‑day stay above the 200‑day? Any major pull‑backs?
- Analyze any missed setups (stocks that broke out after you closed) and note why they were missed.

RISK CONTROL REMINDERS
- Never risk more than 2 % of total equity on a single trade.
- Keep total portfolio risk (sum of all stops) ≤ 10 % of equity.
- If the market VIX rises > 30, tighten stops by an additional 1‑2 %.
- In a choppy market, reduce position size to ¼ of the usual quarter (i.e., ½ of a quarter).

MINDSET REINFORCEMENT (Mark style)
- “The chart is the only thing that gives me comfort.” Review the chart before any emotional decision.  
- Treat each trade like a poker hand – focus on odds, not on the outcome of any single hand.  
- Remember: you are aiming to make more than you lose, over many trades, not to catch every “NVIDIA.”  
- If you feel pressure, step away, re‑read your trade plan, and act only if the plan still holds.  
- Celebrate small wins; learn from losses without blaming the market.

SECTION 6: EXAMPLES (If Applicable)
================================================================================

EXAMPLE 1 – BURLINGTON (BURL) HANDLE  
- Chart showed tight upper‑range closes, forming a classic high handle.  
- Pivot was drawn at the low of the handle; breakout cleared the pivot with no supply left.  
- Entry at the pivot, stop placed just below the low of the handle.  
- Trade stayed above the 50‑day MA; after three lower lows the trade would have been exited.

EXAMPLE 2 – PHC DAILY PIVOT  
- Stock broke out at 9.45 am, ran +10 % intraday, then fell back to the breakout level by close.  
- Because the breakout was not “clean” (price fell back to the breakout level), the trade was rejected.  
- Lesson: only buy when the breakout stays above the pivot and does not immediately retreat.

EXAMPLE 3 – ELAN (ELAN) BASE DURING BEAR MARKET  
- Tight base formed over 5‑7 weeks, volume contracted, then breakout with strong earnings acceleration.  
- Entry at the breakout pivot; stop placed just below the base low.  
- Trade rode the uptrend as the market turned bullish.

EXAMPLE 4 – LULU DOUBLE BOTTOM  
- Two lows formed, each higher than the previous, with a clean breakout on earnings.  
- Pivot was the low of the second bottom; entry at the breakout, stop just below the first bottom.  
- Demonstrates that double‑bottoms can be treated as VCPs when volume contracts.

SECTION 7: FINAL 10‑POINT CHEAT SHEET
================================================================================

1. ONLY BUY AT OR JUST BELOW THE PIVOT POINT – never chase a post‑spike pull‑back.  
2. PLACE A HARD STOP IMMEDIATELY (4‑6 % below entry or below pivot low).  
3. MOVE STOP TO BREAK‑EVEN AFTER 5‑6 % PROFIT – protect gains early.  
4. START WITH A QUARTER POSITION; ADD ONLY IF THE TRADE IS +5‑6 % AND OTHER SETUPS ARE WORKING.  
5. USE VCP (3‑5 TIGHTENING PULL‑BACKS) AS THE PRIMARY ENTRY FILTER.  
6. KEEP TOTAL PORTFOLIO RISK ≤ 10 % OF EQUITY; SINGLE‑TRADE RISK ≤ 2 %.  
7. FOLLOW THE MARKET TREND TEMPLATE (50‑day > 200‑day, shallow S&P pull‑backs).  
8. TREAT EVERY TRADE AS A PROBABILITY GAME – accept losses, don’t chase winners.  
9. BACKSTOP WITH THE 50‑DAY RULE ONLY WHEN THE STOCK CLOSES BELOW IT FOR 3 WEEKS OR HAS HEAVY VOLUME DOWN.  
10. REVIEW EVERY TRADE DAILY, UPDATE WATCH‑LIST, AND KEEP YOUR “SOLD‑LIST” FOR RE‑ENTRY OPPORTUNITIES.
