SECTION 1: STUDENT NOTES (Clear, Simple, Actionable)
================================================================================

Key Idea 1 – Context is everything  
Why it matters: The same technical pattern can be bullish in an early‑stage uptrend and bearish in a late‑stage trend.  
How to apply: Before you trade, determine which “mountain stage” the stock is in (stage 1‑5) and then apply the appropriate rules.

Key Idea 2 – Base Count (stage counting)  
Why it matters: The number of bases a stock has built tells you how far up the mountain you are and how much upside is likely left.  
How to apply: Count completed VCP bases after the 200‑day moving average turned up. Early (stage 1‑2) = more upside, mid (stage 3) = watch volatility, late (stage 4‑5) = prepare to sell.

Key Idea 3 – VCP (Volatility Contraction Pattern)  
Why it matters: VCPs are high‑probability entry setups when they occur early in a trend.  
How to apply: Look for a tightening price range with decreasing volume, then a breakout above the contraction zone with strong volume.

Key Idea 4 – PE Expansion as a Contextual Cue  
Why it matters: Rapid PE expansion (2‑3×) signals that price is outpacing earnings and the stock may be frothy.  
How to apply: Track the PE before the breakout and after each base. If it jumps from ~20× to 40‑70×, tighten your stop or consider exiting.

Key Idea 5 – Up‑Day/Down‑Day Ratio (70% Rule)  
Why it matters: A high proportion of up days shows strength; a sudden drop below the rule signals exhaustion.  
How to apply: Count up‑days vs down‑days over the last 7‑15 trading days. ≥70% up = hold; falling below = start reducing.

Key Idea 6 – Acceleration & Largest Up/Down Days  
Why it matters: The biggest single‑day move, widest spread, and biggest gap indicate the stock is entering a parabolic phase.  
How to apply: When you see a new “largest up day” followed quickly by a “largest down day on heavy volume,” prepare to sell into strength.

Key Idea 7 – 50‑Day Break‑Even or Better Rule  
Why it matters: It gives a simple, objective exit point that adapts to the stock’s volatility.  
How to apply: Set a stop (5‑8%). Once the 50‑day SMA moves above your entry price, move the stop to just below the 50‑day. Exit only when price closes below the 50‑day.

Key Idea 8 – Lower Lows as Support in Strong Uptrends  
Why it matters: Institutions tend to buy on the first two lower lows; the third lower low often signals exhaustion.  
How to apply: Track weekly (or daily) lower lows. Two lower lows = hold; three lower lows or a close below the 50‑day = sell.

Key Idea 9 – “Dinosaur Footprint” – Largest Down Day on Highest Volume  
Why it matters: Institutional profit‑taking shows up as a massive down move on huge volume.  
How to apply: When you see the biggest daily drop on the highest volume since the move began, exit or sharply reduce position.

Key Idea 10 – Mental Model: Mountain Climbing Analogy  
Why it matters: It reinforces the need to adapt tactics as you ascend (early, mid, late stage).  
How to apply: Constantly ask “Which stage am I on?” and adjust position size, stop placement, and profit targets accordingly.

SECTION 3: TRADING RULES & DO'S/DON'TS
================================================================================

DO:
- Determine the stage (1‑5) before entering any trade.
- Use a VCP breakout with strong volume as an entry signal.
- Keep risk per trade at 5‑7% of account equity.
- Move stop to break‑even once price is above the 50‑day SMA.
- Take partial profits when the trade is 2‑3× your risk.
- Monitor PE expansion; if PE doubles or triples, tighten stops.
- Count up‑days; maintain ≥70% up days before adding.
- Watch for the “largest down day on highest volume” and exit promptly.
- Use lower‑low analysis: after three lower lows, prepare to sell.
- Review the mountain stage each day and adjust tactics accordingly.

DON’T:
- Trade a stock solely because it looks “late stage” without checking context.
- Ignore the 200‑day MA; a breakout below it is a weak signal.
- Hold a position after the price falls below the 20‑day MA shortly after entry.
- Let a trade run past the point where the largest up day is followed by a large down day on heavy volume.
- Rely on PE alone to decide entry; it is only a contextual filter.
- Add to a position when the up‑day ratio falls below 70%.
- Keep a full position after three lower lows without a clear catalyst.
- Trade on ticker symbols supplied by others during curriculum sessions.
- Forget to reset stops after a partial profit is taken.
- Assume that a leader in one cycle will lead the next cycle.

SECTION 4: SETUP IDENTIFICATION GUIDE (Based on Transcript)
================================================================================

Chart Cues
- Price has broken above the 200‑day SMA and is forming a tight range (VCP).
- Volume is decreasing during the contraction and spikes on breakout.
- PE ratio at breakout is modest (15‑25×); watch for rapid rise.

Price / Volume Behavior
- Up‑day count ≥70% over the last 7‑15 days.
- First “largest up day” creates a wide spread or gap upward.
- Within 5‑10 days, a “largest down day” appears on the highest volume since the move began.
- After the breakout, price may pull back to the 20‑day or 50‑day SMA.

Buy Points
- Enter on a clean breakout above the VCP contraction zone with volume > average 2‑3×.
- Confirm with at least 12 of the last 15 days being up (ANTs) if early stage.
- Ensure PE has not expanded more than ~2× from base level.

Confirmation Signals
- Price stays above the 200‑day SMA for at least 3‑5 sessions.
- No lower low formed on the daily chart within the first week.
- Volume on the breakout is higher than the average of the prior 20 days.

Danger Signs / Invalidations
- PE expands >3× from base level.
- Up‑day ratio drops below 60% within two weeks.
- Largest down day on highest volume appears.
- Price closes below the 20‑day MA within 3 days of entry.
- Formation of the third lower low (weekly) or a close below the 50‑day SMA.

SECTION 5: PRACTICAL DAILY ACTION PLAN FOR ME
================================================================================

PRE‑MARKET CHECKLIST
- Scan for stocks that have just broken above the 200‑day SMA.
- Verify VCP contraction (price range narrowing, volume falling).
- Check PE ratio at breakout; note any recent expansion.
- Count completed bases (stage 1‑5) using charting software.
- Run the “70% up‑day” filter on the last 10‑15 days.
- Set alerts for largest up day, gap, and volume spikes.

MARKET HOURS DISCIPLINE
- Enter only on a breakout with volume ≥2‑3× average.
- Immediately place a stop at 5‑8% below entry (or below the 20‑day MA if tighter).
- Monitor real‑time volume; if volume spikes on a down move, consider reducing half the position.
- Keep a running tally of up‑days vs down‑days; if ratio falls below 70%, tighten stop.
- Watch for the “largest up day” and note the date; set a reminder to watch for reversal within the next 5‑7 days.

POST‑MARKET REVIEW TASKS
- Record the stage, entry price, stop level, and PE at entry for each trade.
- Review whether any “dinosaur footprint” (largest down day on high volume) occurred.
- Update the base count for each holding; note any new bases formed.
- Calculate profit/loss and compare to average gain; if trade exceeded average gain by >2× risk, consider taking partial profit.
- Log any rule breaches (e.g., price fell below 20‑day MA) and adjust future criteria.

RISK CONTROL REMINDERS
- Never risk more than 7% of account on a single trade.
- Move stop to break‑even once price is above the 50‑day SMA.
- If two weekly lower lows appear, tighten stop to just below the most recent low.
- On a “largest down day on highest volume,” exit or cut position to ≤20% of original size.

MINDSET REINFORCEMENT (Mark style)
- Ask each morning: “Which mountain stage am I on for each position?”
- Visualize the “turnaround point” – be ready to quit while ahead.
- Remember that PE expansion is a warning, not a ban; stay objective.
- Treat each trade as a probability game; focus on the weight of evidence.
- Keep emotions out: if a rule triggers, act immediately; no second‑guessing.
