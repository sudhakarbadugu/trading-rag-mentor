SECTION 1: STUDENT NOTES (Clear, Simple, Actionable)
================================================================================

KEY IDEA 1 – CATEGORIES & CATALYSTS  
Why it matters: Different industries behave differently (biotech = FDA news, cyclicals = commodity cycles). Knowing the category tells you which catalyst to watch and how to weight fundamentals.  
How to apply: When you scan a stock, first label it (leader, turnaround, institutional favorite, cyclical, commodity, biotech). Then pick the right catalyst (earnings, FDA approval, commodity price move, etc.) before you look at the chart.

KEY IDEA 2 – MARKET LEADERS ARE PRIORITY  
Why it matters: Leaders tend to be taken off the market first during corrections, leaving the “real” leaders still available.  
How to apply: Use relative‑strength (RS) and compare the stock to its sector and the broad index. Target stocks with RS > 25 that are still above their 200‑day moving average.

KEY IDEA 3 – NON‑NEGOTIABLE RULES  
Why it matters: Discipline is the single biggest edge.  
How to apply:  
- The stock must be in a clear up‑trend (price above 200‑day, higher highs & higher lows).  
- A stop‑loss line is set before you enter; if price hits it you are out, no exceptions.

KEY IDEA 4 – FUNDAMENTALS ARE SIMPLE  
Why it matters: Growth traders only need three numbers to confirm a healthy business.  
How to apply: Look for EPS growth, sales growth, and expanding margins. If any of the three is flat or declining, skip the stock.

KEY IDEA 5 – RELATIVE STRENGTH AS A REFERENCE POINT  
Why it matters: In a bear market you need stocks that outperform the market; RS tells you that.  
How to apply: Plot the RS line (or compare price to the index). A rising RS while the market falls = high probability setup.

KEY IDEA 6 – VOLATILITY CONTRACTION PATTERN (VCP)  
Why it matters: VCP is the “line of least resistance.” It shows that supply is drying up and a breakout is likely.  
How to apply:  
- Identify a series of pull‑backs (contractions) that get progressively smaller (≈½ to ⅓ of the prior move).  
- Count the contractions (minimum 2, usually 2‑5).  
- The right‑hand side of the base must tighten to single‑digit % (2‑5% is ideal).  
- Volume must contract sharply during each pull‑back and hit a “volume vacuum” at the tightest point.

KEY IDEA 7 – ITERATIONS & FRATALS  
Why it matters: Smaller VCPs inside a larger VCP (fractals) add probability.  
How to apply: Zoom in on the base; if you see a mini‑VCP that mirrors the larger one, treat the setup as higher quality.

KEY IDEA 8 – SUPPLY & DEMAND DYNAMICS  
Why it matters: Understanding who is buying/selling explains why the pattern works.  
How to apply:  
- Trapped buyers = potential supply.  
- Weak hands exit early; strong institutions absorb them.  
- As volume shrinks, weak‑hand supply disappears → price can explode on minimal demand.

KEY IDEA 9 – POSITION SIZING BASED ON DIAMOND GRADE  
Why it matters: A perfect VCP lets you be aggressive; a sloppy one requires a tighter position.  
How to apply: Grade the pattern (depth, symmetry, contraction count, volume vacuum). The better the grade, the larger the position (up to your max risk per trade).

KEY IDEA 10 – PROBABILITY STACKING  
Why it matters: Each qualifying element multiplies the odds, not adds them.  
How to apply: Verify every non‑negotiable (trend, fundamentals, RS, VCP quality). If any fails, the overall probability drops dramatically.

SECTION 2: MINERVINI TRADING CONCEPTS EXTRACTED
================================================================================

- VCP (Volatility Contraction Pattern) – series of tightening pull‑backs with shrinking volume; 2‑6 iterations, each ½‑⅓ the previous size, right‑side tightness single digits.  
- Trend Template – stock must be above its 200‑day moving average, showing higher highs & higher lows; this is the primary filter.  
- Relative Strength (RS) – a numeric line or comparison to the index; RS > 25 signals outperformance, especially in bear markets.  
- Earnings / Sales / Margins – the three fundamental pillars; all must be growing for a growth‑oriented trade.  
- Category / Catalyst Framework – classify stocks (leader, turnaround, biotech, cyclical, institutional favorite) and match the appropriate catalyst (earnings, FDA, commodity price).  
- Stop‑Loss (Line in the Sand) – set a hard exit price before entry; never move it in favor of the trade.  
- Position Sizing Rules – size based on pattern “grade” (diamond analogy); tighter, cleaner VCP = larger position within max risk per trade.  
- Risk Management – max loss per trade (often 1‑2% of account), stop‑loss placement, staggered stops for volatile stocks.  
- Psychology & Discipline – treat trading like poker: know the math, accept variance, stay committed to the rules.  
- Line of Most Resistance → Line of Least Resistance – draw a line from the pivot point through the base; breakout above this line is the entry.  
- Supply/Demand Mechanics – trapped buyers = supply; weak hands exit early; strong institutions absorb supply, reducing it over iterations.  
- Fractals / Mini‑VCPs – smaller VCPs inside a larger one increase confidence.  
- Market Efficiency Concept – VCP works because markets are not perfectly efficient; inefficiencies appear especially in smaller caps and emerging markets.  
- “Cheat” and “Load Cheat” – lower‑price entry points within the base (not fully detailed in transcript but mentioned as sub‑entries).  
- “Stage 1 vs Stage 2” Uptrend – Stage 2 is a mature uptrend where VCPs are most reliable.

SECTION 3: TRADING RULES & DO'S/DON'TS
================================================================================

DO:
- Only trade stocks that are above their 200‑day moving average and showing a clear up‑trend.  
- Verify EPS, sales, and margin growth before looking at the chart.  
- Use a pre‑set stop‑loss; exit immediately if it is hit.  
- Look for at least two clean contractions; ideal is 2‑5.  
- Ensure the right‑hand side of the VCP tightens to ≤ 10% (preferably 2‑5%).  
- Confirm volume is contracting on each pull‑back and hits a low‑volume “vacuum” at the tightest point.  
- Compare the stock’s RS to the market; RS should be rising while the market falls or flat.  
- Grade the VCP like a diamond; the higher the grade, the larger the position (within your max risk).  
- Record every trade, including why it met or failed each rule.  
- Review charts daily and rehearse the “perfect bill” (ideal VCP) to sharpen pattern recognition.

DON’T:
- Enter a trade if the stock is in a downtrend or below the 200‑day line.  
- Chase a breakout that lacks a VCP foundation.  
- Ignore volume; a breakout on high volume without prior contraction is suspect.  
- Trade a VCP with > 6‑7 iterations or with asymmetrical pull‑backs.  
- Buy a stock with a right‑side contraction > 10% (unless you have a compelling fundamental reason).  
- Overlook the market context; a deep pull‑back in a stock that outperformed the market is a red flag.  
- Move your stop‑loss to “give the trade a chance” after entry.  
- Rely on indicators not taught in the program (e.g., stochastic, RSI) for entry decisions.  
- Trade a stock that looks like a “Mickey Mouse” bill – i.e., poor symmetry, high supply left of the pivot.  
- Assume the VCP guarantees a win; always treat each trade as a probability, not a certainty.

SECTION 4: SETUP IDENTIFICATION GUIDE (Based on Transcript)
================================================================================

1. **Chart Cues**
   - Price above 200‑day moving average, higher highs & higher lows.
   - Relative Strength line trending upward while the market is flat or falling.
   - Clear series of pull‑backs (contractions) on daily or weekly chart.

2. **Contraction Count & Depth**
   - Minimum 2 contractions; typical 2‑5.
   - Each contraction roughly ½‑⅓ the size of the previous one.
   - Right‑hand side (the most recent pull‑back) tightens to ≤ 10% (2‑5% ideal).

3. **Volume Behavior**
   - Volume declines on each pull‑back, creating a “volume vacuum” at the tightest point.
   - Very low volume (often near zero) just before breakout is a strong signal.

4. **Buy Point (Entry)**
   - Draw the line of most resistance from the pivot point through the base.
   - When price closes above this line on a candle/bar with a modest volume surge, enter.
   - If the right‑side tightness is in the low single digits, you may add a “cheat” entry slightly earlier.

5. **Confirmation Signals**
   - Close near the high of the last contraction.
   - Volume pickup on the breakout bar.
   - No new supply (no large sell‑off) on the breakout.

6. **Danger Signs / Invalidations**
   - Stock below 200‑day or making lower lows.
   - RS falling or flat while the market is rising.
   - More than 6‑7 contractions, or asymmetrical pull‑backs.
   - Right‑side contraction > 10% or depth > 50% when the market is not in a deep bear.
   - Volume spikes on the pull‑back (indicates new supply).
   - Breakout fails and price falls back below the line of most resistance.

SECTION 5: PRACTICAL DAILY ACTION PLAN FOR ME
================================================================================

**PRE‑MARKET CHECKLIST**
- Scan for stocks > 200‑day, RS > 25, EPS/sales/margin growth > 20% YoY.  
- Categorize each candidate (leader, biotech, cyclical, etc.).  
- Pull up daily & weekly charts; flag any with visible VCP candidates (≥2 contractions).  
- Note contraction count, % depth, and volume trend in a watch‑list spreadsheet.  
- Set alerts for price approaching the line of most resistance for each flagged stock.

**MARKET HOURS DISCIPLINE**
- Monitor flagged stocks; watch for the price to approach the resistance line.  
- When price closes above the line with a volume uptick, enter at the close (or market‑on‑open next bar).  
- Immediately place stop‑loss at the line of most resistance (or a few ticks below the last low).  
- Size position according to VCP grade (e.g., 1% of account for “good” VCP, 0.5% for “fair”).  
- Avoid adding to a trade if the pattern breaks any rule after entry.  
- Keep a running mental note of supply: if volume spikes on pull‑back, consider exiting early.

**POST‑MARKET REVIEW TASKS**
- Journal every trade: entry, stop, reason it met each rule, exit, P/L.  
- Review charts for any missed VCPs or false signals; update pattern library.  
- Re‑grade any “cheat” entries that worked or failed.  
- Update watch‑list: remove losers, add new candidates that formed VCP overnight.

**RISK CONTROL REMINDERS**
- Max loss per trade = 1% of total account (adjust for volatility).  
- Never move stop‑loss upward; only tighten if you add a new position with a tighter entry.  
- If a trade hits stop, log the reason and move on – no revenge trading.  
- Keep overall exposure to VCP trades ≤ 20% of account at any time.

**MINDSET REINFORCEMENT (Mark style)**
- Treat each trade as a probability, not a certainty.  
- Remember the “train schedule” analogy – if the train is late, the probability of a problem rises.  
- Stay patient; wait for the perfect VCP and the breakout line.  
- Review the “perfect $100 bill” image daily to keep the ideal VCP in your retina.  
- Accept that some days will be flat; discipline beats emotion.

6. **Gold ETF (GLD)** – VCP observed on daily chart; pattern held across multiple timeframes (daily, weekly, monthly). Breakout produced a strong rally despite gold’s long‑term efficiency.

SECTION 7: FINAL 10‑POINT CHEAT SHEET
================================================================================

1. **Trend First** – Only trade stocks above the 200‑day moving average in a clear up‑trend.  
2. **Fundamentals Triple‑Check** – EPS, sales, and margins must all be growing.  
3. **Relative Strength > 25** – Stock must out‑perform the market, especially in bear phases.  
4. **VCP Minimum** – At least 2 clean contractions; each ½‑⅓ the previous size.  
5. **Right‑Side Tightness** – Aim for ≤ 5% contraction; single‑digit is ideal.  
6. **Volume Vacuum** – Volume must shrink on each pull‑back and be near zero at the tightest point.  
7. **Draw the Resistance Line** – Entry is the close above the line of most resistance (line of least resistance).  
8. **Set a Hard Stop** – Place stop at the resistance line (or just below the last low) before you enter; never move it.  
9. **Grade the Diamond** – Better symmetry, fewer contractions, tighter volume = larger position (within max risk).  
10. **Stack Probabilities** – Every rule you satisfy multiplies the odds; a single failure drops the probability dramatically.  

Keep this sheet on your desk; refer to it before every trade.
