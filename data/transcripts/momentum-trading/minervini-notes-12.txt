SECTION 1: STUDENT NOTES (Clear, Simple, Actionable)
================================================================================

KEY IDEA 1 – VALUATION IS SECONDARY FOR HIGH‑GROWTH LEADERS  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Fast‑growing market leaders can trade at sky‑high P/E ratios that look “overvalued” but still have huge upside.  Trying to calculate a “fair” price often misleads you.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Focus on price action, growth momentum and chart criteria first.  Use valuation only as a sanity check after the trade is set up.

KEY IDEA 2 – THE CHEAP TRAP  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Buying a stock just because it is cheap (low P/E, low price) can lock you into a falling leader that keeps getting cheaper, pulling you deeper into loss.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Avoid stocks that are below their 200‑day moving average or in a downtrend, even if the P/E looks attractive.  Look for strong relative strength instead.

KEY IDEA 3 – P/E MOVES WITH EARNINGS, NOT PRICE  
WHY IT MATTERS:  When a stock rallies fast, earnings often grow faster, causing the P/E to fall even as the price rises.  The opposite happens on the downside.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Watch the earnings trend.  A falling P/E during a price rise usually signals accelerating earnings – a good sign.  A rising P/E during a price fall often signals earnings lagging price.

KEY IDEA 4 – FOUR STAGE CYCLE (WYCKOFF/STAN WEINSTEIN)  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Only one stage – Stage 2 (uptrend above the 200‑day) – produces the best compounding returns.  Stages 1, 3, 4 waste time or lose value.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Screen for stocks that are in Stage 2: price above 200‑day, 200‑day itself sloping up for at least 30 days, and relative strength >90 (ideally >95).  Stay out of Stage 1 (flat), Stage 3 (top/sideways), Stage 4 (downtrend).

KEY IDEA 5 – 50/80 RULE (Broken Leader Syndrome)  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Historically, about half of leading stocks will later drop 80 % and the other half will drop 50 % after a big rally.  Expect large pull‑backs; don’t assume a cheap dip is a buying opportunity.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Use tight stop‑losses and position sizing that can survive a 50 % move.  Be ready to exit if the stock falls below the 200‑day or shows a breakdown pattern.

KEY IDEA 6 – INSTITUTIONAL SUPPORT IS CRUCIAL  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Retail buying can only move a stock briefly.  Sustainable uptrends need large, persistent institutional buying.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Look for high volume spikes on up days, institutional block trades, and a clean, rising 200‑day.  Avoid stocks that rely on retail hype alone.

KEY IDEA 7 – RISK IS ALWAYS 100 % FROM ENTRY  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Even a stock that looks “cheap” can go to zero.  The only thing that protects you is proper risk management.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Never risk more than 1‑2 % of account equity on a single trade.  Set stop‑losses at a level that limits loss to that amount, regardless of how attractive the entry looks.

KEY IDEA 8 – BUY THE RIGHT PRICE, NOT THE LOWEST  
WHY IT MATTERS:  The optimal entry is just as the stock begins to move up, not the absolute low.  Buying at the low often means you’re already in a downtrend.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Identify the “base” formation (VCP, cup‑with‑handle, flat base) that is breaking out above the 200‑day.  Enter on the breakout, not after the stock has already fallen.

KEY IDEA 9 – REFERENCE POINTS & NORMAL VS ABNORMAL ACTION  
WHY IT MATTERS:  Knowing what normal price/volume behavior looks like lets you spot abnormal moves that signal danger.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Keep a checklist of normal patterns (steady uptrend, expanding volume on up days).  If you see a sudden gap down, extreme volume on a sell, or the 200‑day turning down, treat it as abnormal and reduce exposure.

KEY IDEA 10 – CONTINUOUS LEARNING & FRAMEWORK  
WHY IT MATTERS:  The market changes; a solid framework (stage analysis, moving‑average rule, RS filter) keeps you disciplined.  
HOW TO APPLY:  Follow the daily routine, review each trade against the framework, and adjust only when the framework tells you to.  Do not let emotions or “gut feelings” override the rules.

SECTION 2: MINERVINI TRADING CONCEPTS EXTRACTED
================================================================================

- VALUATION IS SECONDARY FOR HIGH‑GROWTH LEADERS  
  * Over‑valued P/E does not preclude massive upside when growth is extreme.

- CHEAP TRAP  
  * Buying low‑P/E, down‑trend stocks often leads to deeper losses.

- P/E DYNAMICS  
  * P/E falls when earnings outpace price; rises when price falls faster than earnings.

- FOUR‑STAGE CYCLE (Stage 1‑4)  
  * Stage 1 – Neglect/flat, time‑wasting.  
  * Stage 2 – Uptrend, above 200‑day, best compounding.  
  * Stage 3 – Topping/sideways, time‑wasting.  
  * Stage 4 – Downtrend, loss of value.

- 200‑DAY MOVING AVERAGE RULE  
  * No winning stock has broken out below the 200‑day; stay out of any stock below it.

- RELATIVE STRENGTH FILTER  
  * Aim for RS >90 (ideally >95); avoid stocks below 68.

- 50/80 RULE (Broken Leader Syndrome)  
  * Roughly 50 % of leaders later drop 80 %; the other 50 % drop 50 %.

- INSTITUTIONAL BUYING SIGNALS  
  * Large block trades, expanding volume on up days, sustained 200‑day rise.

- STOP‑LOSS & POSITION SIZING (Risk = 1‑2 % per trade)  
  * Always define max loss before entering.

- BUY THE RIGHT PRICE, NOT THE LOWEST  
  * Enter on breakout from a base, not after a deep decline.

- EARLY TURN / MINIMUM 30‑DAY 200‑DAY UPTREND  
  * If the 200‑day has been rising for at least 30 days, the stock qualifies for an “early turn” entry.

- REFERENCE POINTS (Normal vs Abnormal)  
  * Normal: smooth uptrend, volume confirming moves.  
  * Abnormal: sudden gaps, volume spikes on down moves, 200‑day turning down.

- PSYCHOLOGY – “No safe stock”  
  * Every stock is risky; only the direction (up) makes it “good”.

- SEPA (Specific Entry Point Analysis) – implied throughout: precise breakout entry, tight stop, defined exit.

SECTION 3: TRADING RULES & DO'S/DON'TS
================================================================================

DO:
- Screen only for stocks above their 200‑day moving average.
- Verify the 200‑day has been in an uptrend for at least 30 days.
- Use a relative strength rating of 90 or higher.
- Enter on a clean breakout from a base (VCP, cup‑handle, flat base).
- Size each trade to risk no more than 1‑2 % of total capital.
- Place stop‑losses at the base low or a logical technical level.
- Monitor institutional volume; look for large block trades on up days.
- Review each trade against the Stage 2 criteria before adding.
- Keep a daily checklist and stick to it.
- Treat abnormal price action as a signal to reduce or exit.

DON'T:
- Buy a stock just because its P/E or price looks cheap.
- Trade any stock that is below the 200‑day moving average.
- Enter after a stock has already fallen deep (buying the low).
- Ignore the 50/80 rule; assume a big pull‑back will automatically reverse.
- Let emotions override the defined stop‑loss.
- Trade on analyst recommendations alone.
- Add to a position when the stock is breaking down through the 200‑day.
- Risk more than the predefined % of account on a single trade.
- Assume a “safe” or “quality” company guarantees a safe trade.
- Trade without a clear reference point for normal price behavior.

SECTION 4: SETUP IDENTIFICATION GUIDE (Based on Transcript)
================================================================================

CHART CUES
- Price is above the 200‑day moving average.
- The 200‑day itself is sloping upward for ≥30 days (early turn) or longer.
- Relative Strength (RS) >90 (ideally >95).
- A base formation is visible: VCP, cup‑with‑handle, flat base, or consolidation with shrinking range.
- Volume expands on up‑days, contracts on down‑days.

PRICE / VOLUME BEHAVIOR
- Upward price movement with increasing volume on each breakout candle.
- No large gaps down; any gap down should be treated as abnormal.
- Institutional block trade alerts (e.g., 70‑+ million shares at a price level) are a green light.

BUY POINTS
- Enter on the first close (or intraday break) above the base’s resistance line.
- Confirm breakout with at least 1‑2 % price rise on higher volume.
- Ensure stop‑loss can be placed just below the base low (or below a recent swing low).

CONFIRMATION SIGNALS
- 200‑day still rising after the breakout.
- RS remains high or improves.
- Earnings growth outpacing price (PE falling during the rally).
- No immediate reversal patterns (e.g., bearish engulfing, double top).

DANGER SIGNS / INVALIDATIONS
- Price falls back below the 200‑day.
- 200‑day turns down or flattens.
- PE spikes upward while price stalls (earnings lagging).
- Sudden high‑volume sell‑off or gap down.
- RS drops below 68.
- Breakout fails and price closes back inside the base.

SECTION 5: PRACTICAL DAILY ACTION PLAN FOR ME
================================================================================

PRE‑MARKET CHECKLIST
- Run screen for stocks >200‑day, 200‑day up ≥30 days, RS >90.
- Filter for recent base formations (VCP, cup‑handle, flat base) with volume contraction.
- Check earnings calendar; avoid stocks with upcoming earnings surprises unless you have a clear plan.
- Review news for any macro events that could cause abnormal moves.
- Set alerts for any block‑trade filings on the watchlist.

MARKET HOURS DISCIPLINE
- When a watchlist stock breaks out, verify volume and 200‑day status before entering.
- Place stop‑loss immediately (base low or logical technical level).
- Size position to risk ≤1 % of account equity.
- Monitor trade every 30 minutes; if price approaches stop or shows abnormal volume, consider exiting early.
- Keep a trade journal entry: entry price, stop, target, rationale (Stage 2, RS, base type).

POST‑MARKET REVIEW TASKS
- Record trade outcome (win/loss, % gain/loss, reason for exit).
- Compare actual price action to expected “normal” pattern.
- Note any abnormal events (news, volume spikes) and how you responded.
- Update watchlist: remove stocks that fell below 200‑day or lost RS.
- Review risk metrics: total exposure, max drawdown for the day.

RISK CONTROL REMINDERS
- Never exceed 2 % total account risk on all open positions combined.
- Adjust stop‑loss upward (trail) only after the trade has moved at least 2× risk in your favor.
- If a stock drops 50 % from entry, exit immediately – respect the 50/80 rule.
- Keep cash reserve for new Stage 2 opportunities; avoid being fully invested.

MINDSET REINFORCEMENT (Mark style)
- “No stock is safe; only the direction matters.”
- Remind yourself that you are buying a *trend*, not a company.
- Visualize the race‑car analogy: know what “normal” braking feels like; treat deviations as danger.
- Reaffirm the rule: “Buy the right price, not the lowest price.”
- End of day affirmation: “I followed the framework, protected my capital, and positioned for compounding.”

SECTION 6: EXAMPLES (If Applicable)
================================================================================

YAHOO (1990s) – Traded at ~938 × earnings, looked overvalued, yet exploded 8,000 % over years.  Lesson: high PE can be justified by explosive growth; don’t dismiss on valuation alone.

TASER – Bought at 230 × earnings, later fell to ~250 ×, then doubled; earnings surged, pulling PE down.  Lesson: watch earnings acceleration; PE can fall during a rally.

CROCS – PE ~60 before a 400 % rally; later PE fell as earnings outpaced price.  Lesson: high PE before a breakout can become a better value after earnings catch up.

OLLIES – Entered at PE 150, fell to 80‑100 during run, then stock rose 300 % while PE fell to 48.  Lesson: PE compression during a strong move signals earnings growth.

NVIDIA – Recent 30‑50 % pullbacks within a bull market; still above 200‑day.  Lesson: normal pullbacks are expected; stay in if 200‑day remains up.

ZOOM – Rose to $600, fell to $40‑50 (≈90 % drop).  Lesson: even “good” companies can crash; avoid buying below 200‑day.

LULULEMON – Went through multiple stage cycles; only two Stage 2 windows were viable for long positions.  Lesson: stay disciplined to stage criteria; ignore other phases.
